# Spatiotemporal trends in cropland phenology in the Nigerian sahel derived from MODIS NDVI

**Authors:** <unclear from abstract>
**Venue:** Environmental Research Letters · **Date:** <unclear from abstract> · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/ae628c
**Score:** 9/10
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

Two decades of MODIS NDVI show Nigerian Sahel croplands are starting seasons later (+0.26 days/yr) and growing shorter (−0.25 days/yr), with strong SOS–LOS coupling (r ≈ −0.82).

## Summary

- **Problem:** How cropland phenology is shifting across dryland agricultural systems in sub-Saharan Africa under climate change, and whether those shifts are spatially coherent or heterogeneous.
- **Method:** MODIS MOD13A2 NDVI time series (2000–2022) across 10 Nigerian Sahel states; four metrics (SOS, EOS, LOS, NDVI amplitude); mixed-effects models with pixel- and state-level random effects; MANOVA and cross-metric correlations.
- **Key result:** SOS delayed +0.26 days/yr (cumulative up to +15 days in some states); LOS shortened −0.25 days/yr (>22 days in some states); EOS shows no consistent trend; NDVI amplitude increased slightly (+0.00036/yr); SOS–LOS correlation r ≈ −0.82; within-state heterogeneity accounts for ~40–55% of total variance.
- **Why it matters:** Shortened growing seasons in a food-insecure dryland region signal reduced cropping windows; high within-state variance means aggregate trends mask local vulnerability hotspots that require targeted adaptation.
- **Caveats:** MODIS 1-km resolution may conflate cropland with adjacent vegetation; no direct attribution to specific climate drivers (rainfall timing, temperature); study confined to Nigeria's Sahel — generalizability to broader West African drylands untested.
