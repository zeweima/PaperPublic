# [Seasonal bromate formation in the Arctic snowpack: Implications for the bromine biogeochemical cycle](https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.aea3286)

**Authors:** Stefano Frassati, Elena Barbaro, Giulio Cozzi et al.
**Venue:** Science Advances · **Date:** 2026-04-17 · **DOI:** 10.1126/sciadv.aea3286
**Score:** <unclear from abstract>/10
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Arctic snowpack photochemically oxidizes bromide to bromate (BrO₃⁻), sequestering up to 5% of total bromine as a nonreactive reservoir unaccounted for in current atmospheric models.

## Summary

- **Problem:** The fate of bromine in polar snowpacks is poorly constrained; bromate (BrO₃⁻), the terminal oxidation product of Br⁻, had never been detected or mechanistically explained in snow, creating gaps in ozone-depletion and bromine-explosion models.
- **Method:** Snow pit and surface snow samples collected at Svalbard (GSRS site, Ny-Ålesund) across polar night, twilight, and full-day conditions in 2021–2023, plus glacier pits at HDF and KNG; ion chromatography–ICP-MS for BrO₃⁻ at LOD 8×10⁻³ nM; aerosol samples as controls; quantum chemical calculations (WB97X-D3BJ/def2-TZVP, ORCA, C-PCM water solvation) for both radical and ionic reaction pathways.
- **Key result:** BrO₃⁻/Br⁻ ratio rose 10-fold over 5 days of spring sunlight exposure (0.4 nM → 4.3 nM BrO₃⁻); sunlit surface layers consistently show elevated BrO₃⁻/Br⁻ vs. dark deep layers; aerosol samples contained no detectable BrO₃⁻, ruling out direct atmospheric deposition as the source. BrO₃⁻ accounts for up to 5% of total bromine. Two mechanisms confirmed: (1) radical pathway — HOBr photodissociation → Br• → sequential O₃ oxidation → BrO₃⁻ (barrier 43.7 kcal/mol, overcome by UV); (2) ionic pathway — Br⁻ oxidized by O₃/OH• in brine layers (active in dark, lower efficiency due to 20.7 kcal/mol singlet–triplet crossing barrier).
- **Why it matters:** BrO₃⁻ is a nonreactive bromine sink in snowpacks that current WRF-Chem and halogen-chemistry models ignore; its omission causes overestimation of reactive bromine available for ozone depletion and bromine explosions. Seasonal snowmelt will release this reservoir, with downstream water-quality and biogeochemical implications beyond the Arctic (Antarctica, alpine snowpacks).
- **Caveats:** Kinetic parameters for BrO₃⁻ formation and reduction in natural snow remain unquantified; correlation of BrO₃⁻ with MSA suggests a possible photochemical co-production link that is not yet mechanistically resolved; glacier pit sampling is episodic and cannot capture melt-percolation redistribution fully.
