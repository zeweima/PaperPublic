# [Blue Carbon Vulnerability Revealed by Damming- and Eutrophication-Induced Source Shifts in River-Connected Marshes](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.est.5c14518)

## TL;DR

Damming cuts terrestrial OC inputs to coastal marshes by 70%, eutrophication-driven marine productivity recovers only ~40% of lost accumulation, permanently shifting the blue-carbon sink from terrestrial to marine sources.

## Summary

- **Problem:** River damming and eutrophication both alter organic carbon (OC) delivery to coastal marshes, but how these competing stressors interact to affect blue-carbon stocks is poorly understood.
- **Method:** Sediment isotope and geochemical analysis in river-connected marshes to reconstruct OC source histories before and after damming and nutrient loading changes; quantified terrestrial vs. marine OC contributions over time.
- **Key result:** Damming triggered a pulse of ¹³C-depleted aged terrestrial carbon (briefly 70% of inputs), then terrestrial OC inputs fell 70%; eutrophication-fueled marine productivity subsequently restored ~40% of lost accumulation but permanently shifted dominant OC source from terrestrial to marine.
- **Why it matters:** Widespread river fragmentation combined with declining anthropogenic nutrient inputs could disrupt the coastal blue-carbon sink at global scale; the source composition shift has implications for carbon permanence and sequestration accounting.
- **Caveats:** Findings are based on river-connected marsh case studies; generalizability across different geomorphic settings and nutrient regimes needs verification. Abstract is truncated — full quantitative detail on site locations and temporal span unclear.
