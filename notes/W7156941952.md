# Dew Characterization and its Ecological Effects on Vegetation: A Review

**Authors:** Mengyun Sun, Ashley J. Cao, Cynthia Gerlein-Safdi et al.
**Venue:** Water Resources Research · **Date:** 2026-04-28 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1029/2025wr042646
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Synthesis of 66 dew studies across 105 sites quantifies dew characteristics by vegetation and climate type, and maps leaf-to-ecosystem ecological pathways that remain poorly constrained outside drylands.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Dew is the least-studied non-rainfall water input; no unified understanding exists of how dew formation patterns translate into ecological effects on plants across vegetation types and climates.
- **Method:** Systematic literature review of 66 studies (105 sites, past 30 years) screened from Web of Science; compiled dew frequency, amount (mm/d), and duration by vegetation type (trees, grasses, crops, shrubs) and climatic region (hyper-arid to non-dryland); synthesized mechanistic pathways from leaf to ecosystem scale.
- **Key result:** Dew frequency is highest over crops (60.0%) and lowest over trees (29.5%); dew amount is low and relatively uniform across types (0.09–0.15 mm/d) but ecologically significant in drylands where it can reach 50–64% of annual rainfall during dry seasons. Dew duration peaks in crops (6.1 hr/d) vs. trees (2.0 hr/d). Dew can suppress transpiration by up to 25% of daily water loss and increase net photosynthetic rate of C₃/C₄ plants by 5–240% under drought stress. WUE increases up to 100% for some desert species. Only 11 of 66 studies (16.7%) directly quantified ecological variables; most focused on characterizing dew occurrence.
- **Why it matters:** Dew is a measurable supplementary water source with verifiable carbon, water, and energy flux consequences, especially in drylands facing intensifying drought; integrating dew into LSMs and remote-sensing frameworks (VOD, thermal imaging) could improve dryland productivity projections under climate change.
- **Caveats:** Evidence base is heavily skewed toward the Northern Hemisphere (94.2% of sites); field methods conflate dew with fog deposition and vapor absorption; adaxial/abaxial leaf asymmetry is rarely accounted for; mechanistic vs. empirical model performance differences remain unresolved; global warming projected to reduce dew amount and duration across most water-stressed regions.
