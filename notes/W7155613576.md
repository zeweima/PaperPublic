# Climatology and Trends in Spatial Scales of Extreme Precipitation Over Land in the Contiguous US

**Authors:** Trinish Chatterjee, Danielle Touma, Adam Sobel et al.
**Venue:** Geophysical Research Letters · **Date:** 2026-04-25 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1029/2025gl120662
**Score:** 8/10 <sup>top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR
Over 1980–2024, CONUS extreme precipitation is concentrating into fewer but larger-area events, with eastern US large-area event frequency rising significantly.

## Summary
- **Problem:** Changes in extreme precipitation (EP) intensity and frequency are well-studied, but trends in the *spatial scale* of daily EP events over CONUS land are poorly characterized — a gap that matters for compound flood risk and regional planning.
- **Method:** CPC Unified daily gridded precipitation (0.25° × 0.25°, 1980–2024) combined with a connected-component labeling (CCL) algorithm to identify and size individual EP events (defined as exceeding the local 95th-percentile wet-day threshold). Event sizes computed by clustering contiguous or near-contiguous (<400 km separation) extreme gridcells; trends fit by OLS linear regression across 9 NOAA climate regions.
- **Key result:** EP event sizes follow a logarithmic distribution: the largest ~20% (5%) of events account for ~80% (~40%) of total yearly EP area. Largest-area events (>10⁵ km²) are more intense (exceeding 97.5th percentile on average vs. lower for small events) and peak in spring/fall rather than summer. Small-area EP events are declining significantly in all regions (CONUS total: −3.87 events/year for the smallest bin, p < 0.05). EP days per year (EPD) are declining across most of CONUS, but EP area per EP day (AREA_EPD) is trending significantly upward in nearly all regions, leaving total yearly EP area (AREA_YEARLY) flat in the west but significantly growing in the eastern US — driven by increasing frequency of large-area events (e.g., Northeast: +0.11 large events/year, p < 0.05; yearly EP area trend +29,228 km²/year).
- **Why it matters:** Larger daily EP footprints simultaneously stress multiple watersheds, overwhelm local disaster response, and increase compound flood risk. The eastern US double-threat (rising intensity *and* spatial scale) is not captured by traditional point-based EP metrics, implying current flood-damage and insurance-loss models likely underestimate future exposure. Reduced EP frequency also has implications for short-duration drought and groundwater recharge.
- **Caveats:** CPCU-CONUS is known to underestimate EP intensity and overestimate frequency; time-varying station density may bias small-area event trends (especially in sparse western uplands). Land-only domain clips events that extend offshore, artificially shrinking reported areas for coastal/border regions (Florida, Maine, South Texas most affected). Results need replication in independent gridded products; physical mechanisms driving spatial-scale trends are not identified here.
