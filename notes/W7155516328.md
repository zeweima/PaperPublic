# Evaluating the 1.5°C or 2°C Climate Thresholds: When Could These Thresholds be Surpassed?

**Authors:** Sudhansu Sekhar S Rath, Kenneth Kunkel, David R Easterling et al.
**Venue:** Environmental Research Letters · **Date:** 2026-04-24 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/ae6461
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR
Using observation-based trend extrapolation, the 1.5°C climate threshold will likely be surpassed between 2028–2032, earlier than prior estimates.

## Summary
- **Problem:** Existing estimates of when 31-year centered global mean surface temperature will permanently exceed 1.5°C and 2.0°C Paris Agreement thresholds are uncertain and may be outdated given recent apparent acceleration in warming.
- **Method:** Observation-based extrapolation of historical GMST trends using four approaches: 20-yr and 30-yr linear fits, a 55-yr quadratic fit, and a LOESS-based nonlinear sensitivity test. Anomalies referenced to 1850–1900 baseline.
- **Key result:** 1.5°C threshold exceedance projected as early as 2026, most likely 2028–2032; 2.0°C exceedance ranges from early 2040s to mid-2050s. Quadratic method preferred because it captures both quasi-linear multi-decadal trends and the recent warming acceleration. 2024 set an instrumental record; 2025 dipped below 1.5°C due to El Niño-to-La Niña transition.
- **Why it matters:** Tighter, observation-grounded exceedance windows inform adaptation planning and emissions policy; earlier 1.5°C crossing would compress the window for mitigation to avoid 2.0°C.
- **Caveats:** Key uncertainty is whether the recent acceleration in warming is sustained or transient; internal variability (e.g., ENSO) can temporarily obscure the forced trend; purely observational extrapolation does not account for future emissions pathway changes.
