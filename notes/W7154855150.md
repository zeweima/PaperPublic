# Optimized straw–nitrogen management enhances crop yield, soil carbon sequestration, and economic returns in dryland maize systems of Northwest China

**Authors:** Yunfei Ren, Jiayi Li, Fu Liu et al.
**Venue:** Soil and Tillage Research · **Date:** 2026-04-19 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.still.2026.107228
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Moderate straw return (60%) with conventional N (225 kg N ha⁻¹) optimally balances maize yield, SOC gain, CO₂ emissions, and net economic benefit in semi-arid Loess Plateau dryland systems.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Straw return effectiveness in water- and temperature-limited dryland systems depends on coordinated C–N management, but optimal straw–N combinations for simultaneously maximizing productivity, SOC, and economic-environmental performance are poorly defined.
- **Method:** Four-year (2020–2023) factorial field experiment (3×3 RCBD) on the Loess Plateau, Shaanxi, China. Straw treatments: S1 = 100%, S2 = 60%, S3 = 20% of aboveground biomass returned. N rates: N1 = 225, N2 = 180, N3 = 135 kg N ha⁻¹. Measured grain yield, yield stability (CV, SYI), SOC, cumulative soil CO₂ and N₂O, net ecosystem carbon balance (NECB), life-cycle carbon footprint (CF), and net ecosystem economic benefit (NEEB). Treatments ranked with AHP–entropy weighted TOPSIS across seven indicators.
- **Key result:** S2N1 (60% straw return, 225 kg N ha⁻¹) ranked highest overall: aboveground biomass +8.68% and grain yield +5.89% vs. S1N1; SOC +1.43%; soil CO₂ emissions −8.04%; net economic return +12.11%. Reducing N (N2, N3) cut emissions and carbon footprint but imposed grain yield penalties of 9–12.5% relative to N1, degrading NEEB. Annual carbon footprint across all treatments: 569–1994 kg CO₂-eq ha⁻¹ yr⁻¹; all remained net GHG sources within the LCA boundary.
- **Why it matters:** Partial straw removal (60% return) with adequate N supply simultaneously improves SOC efficiency and farm profitability, providing a scalable low-carbon management pathway for semi-arid rainfed drylands; standalone N reduction is insufficient as a climate strategy where it compromises yield.
- **Caveats:** Single site on calcareous Regosol; four years cannot resolve SOC saturation trajectories. Reactive N losses (NO₃ leaching, NH₃ volatilization) not directly measured. Multi-site validation needed for transferability to contrasting dryland soil–climate regimes.
