## TL;DR

Root-released ROS, not microbial activity, is the primary driver of arsenic speciation shifts in the rice rhizosphere.

## Summary
- **Problem:** Arsenic speciation in paddy rhizospheres governs plant uptake and toxicity, but the relative roles of abiotic (ROS-driven) vs. biotic (microbial) processes were unresolved.
- **Method:** Sterilization and ROS-quenching experiments combined with structural equation modeling to partition abiotic vs. microbial contributions to As speciation across multiple chemical dimensions.
- **Key result:** ROS-driven abiotic processes are the key regulator of As speciation under the studied conditions; microbial contributions cannot be fully excluded given that gene abundance reflects potential, not activity.
- **Why it matters:** Reframes rhizosphere As cycling as primarily abiotic at the root surface, with implications for predicting As mobility and rice grain contamination under flooded conditions.
- **Caveats:** Gene abundance used as microbial proxy does not capture actual activity; sterilization may alter soil chemistry beyond simply removing microbes; findings may be condition-specific.
