# Fractionation and environmental assessment of legacy phosphorus from acidic, organic, and calcareous soils

## TL;DR

Legacy-P chemical form and retention mechanism differ sharply by soil type: humic/fulvic-P dominates acidic soils, residual P dominates organic soils, and Ca/Mg-bound P dominates calcareous soils.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Legacy P has accumulated from excess fertilizer and manure application, but its chemical forms across contrasting soil types are poorly characterized, limiting sustainable management strategies.
- **Method:** Modified Hedley P fractionation on three soil types (acidic, organic, calcareous); soil P saturation ratio (PSR) calculated from Mehlich-3 P and Al, Ca, Fe, Mg concentrations; pH and organic matter also measured.
- **Key result:** Mehlich-3 P as share of total P ranged 7–62% (acidic), 1–8% (organic), 3–47% (calcareous). Acidic soils: humic/fulvic-bound P >42%; organic soils: recalcitrant residual P >62%; calcareous soils: Ca/Mg-bound P >69%. PSR analysis showed Ca/Mg minerals dominate P retention in organic and calcareous soils; Al and Fe minerals contribute equally alongside Ca/Mg in acidic soils.
- **Why it matters:** Soil-type-specific P fractions determine both P availability and environmental risk; Ca/Mg-mineral retention in organic soils is an underappreciated pathway, with implications for P recycling strategies and loss risk assessments.
- **Caveats:** Single-method fractionation (Hedley) has known operationally-defined limitations; spatial extent and number of soil samples per type not specified in abstract; field-scale P loss dynamics not directly measured.
