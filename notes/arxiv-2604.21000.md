# Surfactant effect on collective bubble bursting and aerosol emission

**Authors:** Megan Mazzatenta, Samuel M. Koblensky, Luc Deike
**Venue:** arXiv (physics.flu-dyn) · **Date:** 2026-04-22 · **DOI:** n/a
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Surfactant (SDS) boosts submicron sea spray aerosol production 2–4× at optimal concentration while shutting down supermicron jet-drop emission entirely.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Sea spray aerosol emission functions do not account for organic surfactant content of seawater; the independent effects of surfactant on film-drop vs. jet-drop production mechanisms were unresolved, partly because prior studies lacked simultaneous detailed bubble and aerosol measurements.
- **Method:** Laboratory bubbling-tank experiments using artificial seawater with sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) at 0–50 µM; five concentrations, two bubble size configurations (broad-banded and small-bubble "bubbler"). Bubbles (radii 30 µm–5 mm, shadow imaging) and dry aerosols (40 nm–200 µm, holography + OPS + SMPS) measured simultaneously. Aerosol production efficiency defined as aerosols per bursting bubble, accounting for size-dependent bubble lifetime measured via raft-decay experiments.
- **Key result:** Submicron film-drop efficiency (D_p < 0.5 µm, bubbles R_b = 55–1000 µm) increases 2–4× at 10–15 µM SDS (reaching ~120–150 aerosols per bubble), then declines at 50 µM. Supermicron jet-drop efficiency (D_p > 5 µm, bubbles R_b = 130–2500 µm) decreases monotonically with SDS and is effectively zero at 50 µM. Bubble lifetime increases substantially with surfactant for R_b > 200 µm and must be corrected to avoid inflated efficiency estimates.
- **Why it matters:** Directly links seawater organic contamination level to the two aerosol production mechanisms separately, providing an empirical basis for incorporating organic composition into sea spray emission functions used in climate and atmospheric models — particularly relevant for cloud condensation nuclei and ice nucleating particle parameterizations.
- **Caveats:** SDS is a single model anionic surfactant (mimicking biogenic lipids); results may not generalise to the full diversity of marine organics. Experiments use artificial seawater; no direct validation against open-ocean measurements. Exact efficiency magnitudes are sensitive to the chosen bubble-size and aerosol-size integration ranges.
