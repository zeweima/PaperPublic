## TL;DR

Tundra fire area in northern Canada increased post-2005 due to larger individual fires, with fuel conditions and mean summer temperature (June–September) as dominant controls.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Little is known about the relative influence of climate vs. terrain factors on tundra and northern taiga fire risk in northern Canada, where fire regime shifts threaten carbon stocks and permafrost.
- **Method:** Landsat archive imagery used to manually digitize tundra and northern taiga fire perimeters in northcentral and northwestern Canada, 1985–2023 (n fires unspecified); random forests classification with climate and terrain predictors; geospatial climate-change projections.
- **Key result:** Burned area 2005–2023 exceeded 1985–2004 primarily through growth in individual fire size; random forests identified fuel conditions and mean summer temperature (June–September) as key occurrence predictors; future fire frequency projected to rise under ongoing warming, modulated by landscape characteristics.
- **Why it matters:** Tundra fire increases will accelerate permafrost degradation and carbon release; quantifying landscape-mediated risk enables spatially explicit projections for high-latitude carbon-climate feedbacks.
- **Caveats:** Manual digitization may introduce mapping inconsistencies; random forests variable importance does not imply causation; future projections depend on climate scenario assumptions and do not resolve fuel feedbacks post-fire.
