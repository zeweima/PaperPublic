# [Impact of heterogeneity on scalar flux variance relations across diverse ecosystems](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.agrformet.2026.111167)

**Authors:** Tyler Waterman, Ivana Stiperski, Laura Torres-Rojas et al.
**Venue:** Agricultural and Forest Meteorology · **Date:** 2026-04-17 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.agrformet.2026.111167
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Turbulence anisotropy explains substantial scatter in MOST scalar flux-variance relations for heat, water vapor, and CO₂ across 47 NEON ecosystems, and novel anisotropy-generalized scalings reduce CO₂ MAD by 32–36%.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Monin–Obukhov Similarity Theory (MOST) flux-variance relations for scalars (θ, q, CO₂) systematically deviate from observations, but the relative roles of spatial heterogeneity, turbulence anisotropy, and non-stationarity across a broad ecosystem ensemble are poorly quantified.
- **Method:** 47 NEON eddy-covariance towers spanning forests, grasslands, croplands, tundra, tropical, and arid sites (~6 yr); 5-min and 30-min averaging periods; 1 m resolution airborne remote sensing (LAI, NDWI, albedo) for heterogeneity lengthscales; Reynolds stress tensor decomposed into anisotropy invariants (y_b, x_b); stationarity filtered via a 30% variance-flux ratio metric; new anisotropy-generalized Φ = f(ζ, y_b) curves fit simultaneously across all sites.
- **Key result:** (1) Spatial heterogeneity lengthscale relative to turbulent lengthscale directly predicts elevated Φ (scaling error), especially under unstable conditions. (2) Turbulence anisotropy dependence extends from velocity to all three scalars: very anisotropic turbulence (y_b < 0.1) produces consistently elevated Φ, diverging strongly from traditional curves. (3) Novel anisotropy-generalized scalings achieve Skill Scores of 20.3% (θ, unstable), 6.8% (q, unstable), 32.2% (CO₂, unstable), and 35.8% (CO₂, stable) over traditional MOST. (4) CO₂ scaling deviations correlate with site bioactivity; arid, low-flux sites show the largest errors. (5) Non-stationarity amplifies errors near-neutral but anisotropy partially captures non-stationary behavior.
- **Why it matters:** Improved surface-layer parameterizations for ESMs and NWP; anisotropy-based scalings enable better flux-variance inversion measurements without requiring expensive infrared gas analyzers at every site; explains why CO₂ and moisture scaling "fail" differently across ecosystems.
- **Caveats:** Heterogeneity lengthscales computed from static remote sensing flights (annual, fixed wind direction); footprint-varying heterogeneity not resolved. Very anisotropic (one-component) turbulence at sites like SOAP remains poorly fit by any scaling. Coastal sites (PUUM, GUAN) likely affected by advective flows not captured by MOST-framework corrections.
