# [More eddying of subtropical western boundary currents boosts stratification and cools shelf seas](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41558-026-02599-9)

**Authors:** Kathryn L. Gunn, Lisa M. Beal
**Venue:** Nature Climate Change · **Date:** 2026-04-15 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41558-026-02599-9
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Intensifying Agulhas Current eddies converge heat/salt toward the current core, cooling adjacent South African shelf seas and boosting stratification — even without any change in current volume transport.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Western boundary currents are eddying more with climate change, but the net effect of cross-shelf eddy fluxes on adjacent shelf and slope seas has been unquantified from in-situ observations.
- **Method:** 26-month mooring time series (7 moorings, 34°S) from the Agulhas System Climate Array; barotropic and baroclinic energy conversion rates computed from velocity, temperature, and salinity covariances; eddy fluxes decomposed into divergent (irreversible) and rotational components; current separated into ~90% linear (frontal eddy) and ~10% meandering states.
- **Key result:** Net cumulative eddy heat and salt flux is directed toward the current core — eddies cool inshore shelf waters while broadening and stratifying the current. On the inshore cyclonic edge, ~40 submesoscale (10 km) frontal instabilities per 26 months drive baroclinic conversion at (4±2)×10⁻⁸ m²s⁻³, pumping cold nutrient-rich water onto the shelf. Offshore 100-km meanders dominate the anticyclonic side, stirring heat and salt along isopycnals and enhancing stratification. Surface waters warm at 3–4× the mean-ocean rate; depth waters cool. Frontal eddies account for two-thirds of the cumulative inshore eddy heat flux.
- **Why it matters:** Provides a mechanistic explanation for simultaneous rapid surface warming and reduced poleward heat transport in the Agulhas system, and predicts that all subtropical western boundary currents will drive increasing shelf-sea extremes as eddying intensifies — independent of whether volume transport holds steady. Increased upwelling may boost coastal productivity but intensify ecological extremes.
- **Caveats:** Observations limited to a single latitude (34°S); upper-water-column gaps during instrument blow-down introduce uncertainty in near-surface eddy fluxes (σ_T ≈ 0.14°C, σ_S ≈ 0.05 psu); 2D mooring array cannot close the full Lorenz energy cycle; generalization to other western boundary currents assumes similar eddy energetics and topographic slopes.
