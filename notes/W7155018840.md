# Seasonal reversal of river–groundwater exchange controls dissolved organic matter dynamics across the arid margin of the Tibetan Plateau

**Authors:** Bing Yi, Jingtao Liu, Yong‐Sik Ham et al.
**Venue:** Water Research · **Date:** 2026-04-20 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.watres.2026.125985
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Seasonal reversal of surface water–groundwater exchange in an arid Tibetan Plateau basin acts as an on/off switch governing dissolved organic matter sources, composition, and transport.

## Summary

- **Problem:** How seasonal reorganization of river–groundwater exchange controls DOM dynamics in high-altitude arid endorheic basins remains poorly characterized, limiting carbon budget assessment in climate-sensitive inland systems.
- **Method:** Golmud River Basin, Qaidam Basin, northern Tibetan Plateau; 80 groundwater + 31 surface water samples (high-flow) and 81 GW + 12 SW samples (low-flow). Combined stable isotopes (δ²H, δ¹⁸O), UV-Vis and EEM-PARAFAC (5-component model), Bayesian isotope mixing (MixSIAR), XGBoost-SHAP feature importance, multiscale geographically weighted regression (MGWR), and structural equation modeling (SEM).
- **Key result:** SGI-related fluxes contributed 36% of DOM to the pool, exceeding plant decomposition (18.6%), wastewater (18.6%), and soil inputs (16.9%). During high-flow, evaporation/precipitation dynamics explained 47.8% of groundwater DOM restructuring; during low-flow, groundwater level was the primary driver (49.7%). Storm flushing rapidly transported fresh protein-like C3 into headwater groundwater; low-flow biogeochemical control transformed allochthonous precursors into bio-stable autochthonous forms (C2, C4). MGWR outperformed GWR and global regression (R² 0.89–0.92) across all flow conditions.
- **Why it matters:** Demonstrates that SW-GW exchange direction—not just magnitude—is the master variable governing carbon character in arid inland basins; provides a process framework for predicting DOM evolution under accelerating Tibetan Plateau warming and for water-quality management in similar endorheic systems.
- **Caveats:** Single basin with limited anthropogenic disturbance; low-flow surface water sample size small (n = 12); vertical separation distance and deeper aquifer dynamics not addressed; findings may not transfer directly to dam-regulated or agriculturally dominated basins.
