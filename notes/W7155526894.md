# Soil phosphorus availability drives decade-scale stability across life stages in a subtropical forest

**Authors:** Ganxin Feng, Dong Dai, Yi Zheng et al.
**Venue:** Science Advances · **Date:** 2026-04-24 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.aec5351
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Inorganic P promotes seedling diversity and stability but suppresses adult stability; organic P consistently reduces both, revealing life-stage-dependent P effects in a subtropical forest.

## Summary

- **Problem:** How different chemical forms of soil N and P regulate tree diversity and temporal stability across seedling vs. adult life stages is unknown — most prior work either focuses on grasslands or a single life stage.
- **Method:** 50-ha permanent forest plot in Heishing Nature Reserve, southern China; 31,636 seedlings (2008–2023, annual) and 148,845 adults (5-year censuses since 2011); 1,888 soil samples kriged to high-resolution maps of inorganic and organic N and P; piecewise structural equation models and neighborhood competition models partitioning population stability, species asynchrony, richness, and evenness.
- **Key result:** Seedling stage — diversity-stability driven by species richness (R² = 0.498 for community stability); inorganic P enhanced seedling richness and community stability (SEM standardized total effect = +0.207) via reduced interspecific competition; organic P suppressed richness and stability (total effect = −0.070). Adult stage — diversity-stability driven by evenness (R² = 0.557 with asynchrony); inorganic P reduced adult asynchrony and community stability (total effect = −0.135) by eliminating interspecific competition and collapsing compensatory dynamics; organic P again reduced diversity and stability (total effect = −0.056). Organic and inorganic N effects were consistently weaker than P across both stages.
- **Why it matters:** P form and tree life stage jointly determine the sign of nutrient-stability relationships — inorganic P can be simultaneously stabilizing for seedlings and destabilizing for adults, undermining single-life-stage assessments used in forest management and global change projections. Results argue for distinguishing P fractions and ontogenetic stage in forest nutrient policies.
- **Caveats:** Soil nutrients measured once (2017/2023 snapshots), so temporal dynamics of nutrient availability are uncharacterized; stability metrics differ in temporal resolution between stages (annual vs. 5-year); SMT-inorganic P includes partially occluded fractions, attenuating bioavailability signals; mycorrhizal and microbial pathways inferred but not directly measured.
