# Soil phosphorus availability drives decade-scale stability across life stages in a subtropical forest

**Authors:** Ganxin Feng, Dong Dai, Yi Zheng et al.
**Venue:** Science Advances · **Date:** 2026-04-24 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.aec5351
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR
Inorganic soil P promotes seedling stability via competition alleviation but suppresses adult stability by eliminating species asynchrony, reversing its effect across life stages.

## Summary
- **Problem:** How different chemical forms of soil N and P regulate tree diversity and temporal community stability across seedling vs. adult life stages in forests is unknown, as most stability studies focus on a single life stage.
- **Method:** Long-term forest dynamics plot (50 ha) at Heishiding Nature Reserve, southern China; 31,636 seedlings and 148,845 adults tracked over ~12 years; high-resolution kriged soil nutrient maps from 1,888 samples; piecewise structural equation modeling and neighborhood competition models linking inorganic/organic N and P fractions to diversity components and community stability.
- **Key result:** Seedling stability is driven by species richness (R² = 0.498 with community stability), while adult stability is driven by evenness (R² = 0.557). Inorganic P positively predicted seedling stability (standardized total effect = +0.207) but negatively predicted adult stability (total effect = −0.135) by suppressing species asynchrony. Organic P consistently reduced diversity and stability at both stages (total effects −0.070 seedlings, −0.056 adults). Soil N effects were comparatively weak and largely non-significant for community stability. Seedling community stability explained variance rose to R² = 0.87 in SEM; adult to R² = 0.64.
- **Why it matters:** Demonstrates that P form — not just total P — determines the sign and pathway of nutrient effects on forest stability, and that management targeting a single life stage misses life-stage reversals. Inorganic P enrichment (e.g., from weathering gradients or fertilization) could destabilize mature forest communities even while stabilizing regeneration.
- **Caveats:** Soil nutrients measured at a single time point (snapshot, not temporal); organic P is a residual (total minus inorganic) pool that may aggregate multiple fractions including less-labile forms; mycorrhizal and microbial mediators inferred but not directly measured; reverse-causality partially addressed via SAR models but not fully resolved; results from one subtropical site may not generalise to boreal or tropical forests.
