# China's lake expansion amplified rapid CO₂ emissions

**Authors:** Shuailong Feng, Shuai Zhang, Haijian Bing et al.
**Venue:** Science Advances · **Date:** 2026-04-22 · **DOI:** 10.1126/sciadv.aea4657
**Score:** <unclear from abstract>/10
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

China's lakes increased CO₂ emissions from 11.25 to 13.94 Tg C yr⁻¹ (2000–2021), driven largely by lake area expansion at 71–462 km² yr⁻¹ and eutrophication.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Long-term dynamics of lake CO₂ emissions across China are poorly constrained; prior estimates relied on static flux rates and coarse lake-area snapshots, missing temporal variability and size-dependent processes.
- **Method:** Compiled 1,588 in situ FCO₂ records from 232 lakes; trained a random forest (RF) model using 11 watershed-scale environmental and socioeconomic predictors to estimate daily FCO₂ (R² = 0.84 training, R² = 0.79 test); integrated with annual lake-area dynamics (GSWM, 30-m resolution) for 2000–2021 across five regional lake zones.
- **Key result:** National lake CO₂ emissions rose from 11.25 Tg C yr⁻¹ in 2000 to 13.94 Tg C yr⁻¹ in 2021, with an abrupt post-2010 increase (+0.279 Tg C yr⁻¹ gain 2010–2021). Large lakes (>50 km²) dominated, contributing 62% of emissions. FCO₂ varied 4–48% across regions in response to climate extremes. Lake emissions offset ~12% of national wetland carbon sinks. Human factors (GDP dominant) explained 66.3% of interannual CO₂ variability; climate factors 32.6%.
- **Why it matters:** Lake expansion and eutrophication are turning China's lakes into intensifying CO₂ sources; this 12% offset of wetland carbon sequestration is policy-relevant for national carbon budgets and mitigation targets.
- **Caveats:** RF model lacks explicit water-quality and macrophyte data, limiting mechanistic representation of shallow/vegetated lakes; spatial sampling biases in the training dataset; riparian zones, rice paddies, and wetlands excluded; small thermokarst lakes on Tibetan Plateau under-represented.
