## TL;DR

Sub-period calibration with all-parameter dynamic schemes (Experiments 5 & 7) achieves median NSE and LNSE ~0.7–0.8 across 219 MOPEX catchments, outperforming static calibration (median NSE 0.4–0.5).

## Summary

- **Problem:** Static (time-invariant) parameter calibration averages over intra- and inter-annual catchment variability, degrading simulation accuracy across flow regimes—especially extreme high and low flows—in dynamically changing catchments.
- **Method:** Seven calibration experiments applied to HYMOD on 219 MOPEX catchments (1983–2000); experiments vary objective function configuration (Experiments 1–3, time-invariant) and dynamic parameterization scheme (Experiments 4–7). Dynamic catchment characteristics are extracted via a climatic + land-surface index system, screened with MIC, reduced with PCA, and clustered into sub-periods (mean 4.2 per catchment) using Fuzzy C-Means. SCE-UA is the optimizer throughout. Experiment 7 adds smooth parameter transitions at sub-period boundaries during evaluation to prevent abrupt state discontinuities.
- **Key result:** Static schemes (Experiments 1–3) yield median NSE 0.4–0.5 with frequent negative LNSE. Single dynamic parameter (Experiment 4) raises median NSE/LNSE to ~0.55/0.8. Full multi-parameter dynamic calibration (Experiments 5 & 7) reaches median NSE and LNSE ~0.7–0.8, with more compact interquartile ranges. Experiment 7's smooth-transition strategy reduces boundary discontinuities versus Experiment 6's abrupt switching, recovering low-flow LNSE robustness. Composite FDC-based objective functions (Experiment 3) paradoxically reduced NSE/LNSE relative to baseline by overfitting extreme quantiles.
- **Why it matters:** Provides a reproducible, model-agnostic diagnostic framework (applicable to lumped, semi-distributed, and fully distributed models) for embedding dynamic catchment characteristics into calibration; directly relevant to non-stationary catchments under vegetation change, land use shifts, or climate variability.
- **Caveats:** Tested on a single conceptual model (HYMOD); parameter improvements may partly compensate structural deficiencies rather than representing genuine physical change. High-dimensional dynamic calibration increases computational cost. Framework tested on US catchments only (1983–2000); generalizability to data-sparse or heavily human-modified basins is unconfirmed.
