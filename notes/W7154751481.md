# [A weakened diurnal weather constraint leads to longer burning hours in North America](https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.aed0725)

**Authors:** Kaiwei Luo, Xianli Wang, Dante Castellanos-Acuna et al.
**Venue:** Science Advances · **Date:** 2026-04-17 · **DOI:** 10.1126/sciadv.aed0725
**Score:** <unclear from abstract>/10
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Climate-driven weakening of day-night weather constraints expanded North American potential burning hours 36% over 1975–2024, with extreme-burning days rising 81–233% in fire-prone biomes.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Intraday wildfire dynamics (hourly burning duration and intensity) are poorly quantified at continental scale; broader fire regime metrics miss the shift toward erratic, overnight burning that overwhelms suppression.
- **Method:** GOES-R geostationary active fire detections (~5–15 min temporal resolution) for 8,993 fires >200 ha across North America (2017–2023) characterized observed active burning hours (ABH) and fire radiative power (FRP). A random forest (RF) classifier trained on ERA5 + Canadian Fire Weather Index System (CFWIS) variables modeled hourly burning probability; this model was then applied to ERA5 fields 1975–2024 at 0.25° to reconstruct potential burning hours (PBH) continent-wide. Logistic regression provided a robustness check; field significance assessed via block permutation (1,000 permutations, 3-year blocks).
- **Key result:** Annual PBH rose ~36% over 50 years (Mann-Kendall P < 0.05 for 52% of burnable area); 44% of burnable areas gained +26.2 potential active days and 34% gained +1.2 PBH per active day. Extreme days (≥12 PBH) rose 81% and ≥24 PBH days rose 233% in boreal tundra woodland west. Western regions and spring/fall seasons showed the steepest gains (spring 57%, fall 48% relative increase). In observed fires, ~60% peaked in intensity within 24 h of detection and 14% of active days peaked at night.
- **Why it matters:** Longer and more nocturnal burning directly degrades suppression windows, accelerates fire spread, and compresses evacuation timelines. The PBH framework separates weather-driven burning potential from fuel and ignition effects, providing an operationally useful hourly metric for fire management and simulation model inputs.
- **Caveats:** PBH captures weather pathway only — fuel build-up, ignition patterns, and suppression capacity are not encoded. GOES-R coverage gaps (Alaska/Yukon pre-2018, sensor VZA biases) likely underestimate ABH for small/cool and high-latitude fires. Historical ERA5 feature space is nearly enveloped by the training period (median 99.9% coverage), limiting extrapolation risk but not eliminating it.
