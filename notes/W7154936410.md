# A millennium of cold-water coral habitat loss in the East Pacific during low ENSO variability in the mid- to late Holocene

**Authors:** <unclear from abstract>
**Venue:** PNAS · **Date:** 2026 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2532081123
**Score:** 8/10
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

A ~5,000-year-old millennial gap in cold-water coral occurrence at the Galápagos coincides with reduced ENSO variability and a persistent La Niña-like state, implicating intensified oxygen minimum zones as the kill mechanism.

## Summary

- **Problem:** ENSO evolution under past climate change and its influence on deep-sea ecosystems is poorly constrained; the response of cold-water corals to prolonged La Niña-like conditions had not been examined.
- **Method:** U-Th dating of >900 subfossil scleractinian corals from the Galápagos Archipelago spanning depths to ~1,000 m; coral occurrence timeseries compared against paleoclimate ENSO reconstructions across the Holocene.
- **Key result:** Corals inhabited the region for at least 117,000 y but show a millennial-scale absence ~5,000 y ago, coinciding with suppressed ENSO cyclicity and persistent La Niña-like conditions; coral populations recovered as ENSO variability strengthened toward modern levels.
- **Why it matters:** Demonstrates that deep-sea coral habitats are sensitive to centennial–millennial ENSO shifts via upwelling-driven oxygen minimum zone expansion, providing a paleo-benchmark for projecting cold-water coral vulnerability under future ocean warming and altered ENSO.
- **Caveats:** Oxygen minimum zone intensification as kill mechanism is hypothetical; direct paleo-oxygen proxy data from the habitat depth not reported; single-archipelago record may not generalize to other East Pacific cold-water coral sites.
