# Human footprint on estuarine tidal hydrodynamics

**Authors:** Joris Beemster, Stefan A. Talke, Dirk S. Van Maren et al.
**Venue:** Nature Geoscience · **Date:** 2026-04-24 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41561-026-01969-4
**Score:** 9/10 <sup>top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Across 25 estuaries worldwide, dredging and land reclamation over two centuries amplified tidal ranges, accelerated propagation, and shifted asymmetry — effects exceeding sea-level rise in magnitude and peaking >100 km inland.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Human interventions (dredging, land reclamation, barrier construction) reshape estuarine geometry, yet no global comparative analysis had quantified how they alter tidal amplitude, propagation speed, and asymmetry from coast to tidal limit across decadal–centennial timescales.
- **Method:** Archival nautical charts, digitized bathymetric maps, hydrographic surveys, and tide gauge records from 25 morphodynamically active estuaries across Europe, Asia, and North America. Along-channel tidal range profiles compared between earliest available records (19th century in most European systems) and modern observations. Simplified analytical shallow-water model used to attribute changes to channel deepening, planform area loss, and sea-level rise.
- **Key result:** 20 of 25 estuaries show inland amplification of tidal range beyond the historical maximum location. Average channel depth increased from 6.0 to 8.3 m (+38%; σ = ±2.3 m), with some systems more than doubling (Seine +101%, Elbe +104%, Coos Bay +117%, Rhine-Meuse +144%, Loire +169%). Wave celerity increased in 22 of 24 systems by an average of 2.03 m s⁻¹ (+38%). Sea-level rise contributions at estuary mouths (−1.3 to 2.0 mm yr⁻¹) are minor relative to hydrodynamic changes driven locally. The point of maximum tidal change lies far inland — often >100 km — consistent with human interventions as the dominant signal rather than coastal sea-level forcing. Planform area decreased 21% ± 20% on average, with supratidal zones declining 49% ± 35%.
- **Why it matters:** Anthropogenic modifications have historically outpaced sea-level rise as the driver of estuarine water-level change; this reframes attribution of flood risk, salt intrusion, and sediment transport shifts. Wetland restoration and managed realignment are identified as nature-based strategies to damp tidal energy and improve resilience ahead of further sea-level rise.
- **Caveats:** Sample biased toward large, port-associated estuaries with long tide gauge records; lagoonal, fjord, and wave-dominated systems excluded. Only two bathymetric snapshots per estuary — ongoing morphodynamic adjustment means reported changes may underestimate full equilibrium response. Analytical model cannot resolve nonlinear tidal reflections or complex channel networks.
