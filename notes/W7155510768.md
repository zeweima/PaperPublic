# Human footprint on estuarine tidal hydrodynamics

**Authors:** Joris Beemster, Stefan A. Talke, Dirk S. Van Maren et al.
**Venue:** Nature Geoscience · **Date:** 2026-04-24 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41561-026-01969-4
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR
Across 25 global estuaries, dredging and land reclamation amplified tidal ranges and accelerated tidal propagation more than sea-level rise, with largest changes >100 km inland.

## Summary
- **Problem:** Global sea-level rise is widely assumed to dominate long-term changes in estuarine water levels, but the relative contribution of local human interventions (dredging, land reclamation) versus external forcing had not been systematically quantified across many estuaries.
- **Method:** Archival nautical charts, bathymetric surveys, digitized tide gauge records, and modern observations from 25 morphodynamically active estuaries worldwide (Europe, Asia, North America), spanning decadal to centennial timescales. Along-channel profiles of tidal range, wave celerity, and tidal asymmetry were extracted; a simplified linearized shallow-water analytical model was used to attribute changes to channel deepening, area loss, and sea-level rise.
- **Key result:** In 20 of 25 systems, the maximum tidal range increase occurred inland of the historical peak, with average channel depths rising from 6.0 to 8.3 m (+38%). Tidal wave celerity increased in 22 of 24 systems by an average of 2.03 m s⁻¹ (+38%). Total estuarine planform area decreased by 21% ± 20%, with supratidal zones declining 49% ± 35%. MTL change rates varied from −9.5 to +10.5 mm yr⁻¹, exceeding local sea-level rise rates (−1.3 to 2.0 mm yr⁻¹) at estuary mouths. Analytical model results show channel deepening is the dominant driver of regime shift, with area loss playing a secondary role; sea-level rise contribution is minor by comparison.
- **Why it matters:** Anthropogenic modifications historically dominated over sea-level rise in driving estuarine water level trends; tide gauge records near estuary mouths therefore underestimate actual inland water level changes. This reframes flood risk attribution and suggests that nature-based restoration (wetland reintroduction, managed realignment) could partially reverse amplification and build resilience to future climate change.
- **Caveats:** Sample is biased toward large, well-documented port estuaries in Europe and North America; lagoonal, fjord, and wave-dominated systems are excluded. Only two time snapshots are compared per estuary, missing transient dynamics. Geometric variable uncertainties (planform area) can be larger than hydrodynamic uncertainties. River discharge changes and nonlinear tidal interactions are not fully resolved by the analytical model.
