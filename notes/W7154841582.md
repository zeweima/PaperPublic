# Air Quality Penalty in Southeast Asia Driven by AMOC Slowdown

**Authors:** Ryan Vella, Benedikt Steil, Virna Meccia et al.
**Venue:** Geophysical Research Letters · **Date:** 2026 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1029/2025gl121309
**Score:** 8/10
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

A 60% AMOC slowdown weakens the Asian monsoon, suppresses wet deposition, and raises surface PM₂.₅ by 22% and O₃ by 10% over Southeast Asia.

## Summary

- **Problem:** AMOC slowdown impacts on physical climate are well-studied, but consequences for atmospheric chemistry and air quality—especially in densely populated tropical regions—remain unquantified.
- **Method:** ECHAM/MESSy Atmospheric Chemistry-Climate Model (EMAC) at T63L31 (~1.9° × 1.9°, 31 levels) with fully interactive chemistry, aerosol–cloud interactions, and dynamic vegetation (LPJ-GUESS). Boundary condition perturbations derived from a 140-year EC-Earth3 freshwater hosing simulation that reduces AMOC from ~17.5 Sv to ~7.0 Sv (60% decline). Time-slice design contrasting present-day (2015) control against the hosing state; 10-year analysis period after 2-year spin-up.
- **Key result:** Over Southeast Asia, annual precipitation drops ~58% (monsoon season June–September down 504 mm, −51%); Monsoon Precipitation Index falls from 0.74 to 0.65. Reduced convection and cloud cover increase surface OH ~20%, surface O₃ ~10% (in-situ chemical production rate +23%; NO₂ photolysis rate +33%). Total aerosol optical thickness increases across Southeast Asian landmasses; organic aerosol burden near surface rises ~60%; regional mean annual surface PM₂.₅ increases from 17.7 to 21.6 µg m⁻³ (+3.9 µg m⁻³, +22%). Population-weighted PM₂.₅ exposure nearly triples: 10.3 → 27.6 µg m⁻³ over populous grid cells exceeding 16 µg m⁻³. Mid-to-upper troposphere OH decreases ~20% due to reduced lightning NOₓ and weaker convective transport.
- **Why it matters:** Establishes a mechanistic teleconnection pathway by which North Atlantic ocean circulation tipping-point behavior can degrade air quality ~10,000 km away, potentially offsetting local emission-control benefits. Public health implications are severe for one of the world's most pollution-exposed populations.
- **Caveats:** Prescribed SSTs suppress thermodynamic aerosol-forcing feedback on the ocean; fire emissions are prescribed (interactive fires under drier conditions would amplify PM₂.₅ further); single chemistry-climate model; does not account for combined AMOC weakening + background global warming trajectory.
