# Amazon forest loss: An all-sky biophysical top-of-atmosphere cooling feedback

**Authors:** Tom Dror, Graham Feingold
**Venue:** Science · **Date:** 2026-04-23 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1126/science.adz8296
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR
Amazon deforestation triggers a TOA cooling of up to 6.8 W m⁻² in high-loss areas, with cloud-driven albedo increases doubling the surface-brightening effect alone.

## Summary
- **Problem:** The full biophysical impact of Amazon deforestation on Earth's energy balance — particularly the role of cloud feedbacks in mediating top-of-atmosphere (TOA) radiation — has remained poorly constrained.
- **Method:** Two decades of multisource satellite observations were used to isolate biophysical signals tied to forest loss fraction, integrating surface and atmospheric (including cloud) effects into an all-sky TOA radiative feedback estimate.
- **Key result:** In high-loss areas, shortwave TOA cooling reaches 6.8 ± 0.6 W m⁻²; cloud-driven albedo increases account for roughly half of this, doubling the effect relative to surface brightening alone. Both shortwave and longwave TOA fluxes show cooling that scales with forest loss fraction, with shortwave dominating.
- **Why it matters:** Conventional deforestation climate assessments focus on biogeochemical warming (CO₂ release) and surface albedo, often concluding net warming. This observational result shows that cloud-mediated biophysical feedbacks introduce a significant regional cooling countereffect, altering the net radiative balance picture and implying current models may underestimate this compensating mechanism.
- **Caveats:** Results are observational and regional; causal attribution of the cloud response specifically to deforestation (vs. other land-use or meteorological drivers) requires further modeling. Long-term or global extrapolation is uncertain. The paper is paywalled and deeper methodological details (e.g., how forest loss fraction is defined, satellite products used) could not be verified from the abstract alone.
