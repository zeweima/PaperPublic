# Amazon forest loss: An all-sky biophysical top-of-atmosphere cooling feedback

**Authors:** Tom Dror, Graham Feingold
**Venue:** Science · **Date:** 2026-04-23 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1126/science.adz8296
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

Amazon deforestation produces net TOA cooling of up to 6.8 W m⁻² in shortwave, with cloud-driven albedo increases doubling the effect of surface brightening alone.

## Summary

- **Problem:** The full biophysical climate impact of Amazon deforestation — especially cloud-mediated effects — is poorly constrained, leaving a gap between surface energy balance estimates and actual top-of-atmosphere (TOA) radiative forcing.
- **Method:** Two decades of multisource satellite observations over the Amazon; biophysical signals of forest loss isolated statistically; all-sky TOA shortwave and longwave fluxes related to forest loss fraction.
- **Key result:** In high-loss areas, shortwave TOA cooling reaches 6.8 ± 0.6 W m⁻²; cloud-driven albedo increases account for ~half of this, doubling the effect relative to surface brightening alone. Both shortwave and longwave fluxes scale with forest loss fraction, with shortwave dominating.
- **Why it matters:** Demonstrates that cloud feedbacks — not just surface albedo — are the dominant biophysical response to deforestation; current climate models and land-management policies that omit cloud responses substantially underestimate this cooling signal.
- **Caveats:** Observational study cannot fully attribute causality; 20-year satellite record may not capture long-term equilibrium responses; cooling signal does not negate other deforestation harms (carbon release, precipitation changes).
