# Bidirectional feedbacks between Abies koreana dieback and soil properties slow soil carbon turnover in a subalpine forest

**Authors:** Hyunshin Koh, Yea-Na Han, Chanoh Park et al.
**Venue:** Biogeochemistry · **Date:** 2026-04-27 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10533-026-01332-0
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

Korean fir dieback on nutrient-poor, sandy slopes feeds back to slow soil organic matter decomposition via reduced rhizosphere priming and older, higher C:N carbon stocks.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Tree dieback is increasing globally in subalpine conifers, but how site-specific soil properties both predispose trees to dieback and are subsequently altered by it — creating bidirectional feedbacks on soil carbon turnover — is poorly understood.
- **Method:** Soils sampled at three depths (0–5, 5–10, 10–15 cm) across nine stands on Mt. Jiri, South Korea, split into high-mortality (HM) and low-mortality (LM) sites; compared mineralogy, texture, C:N, δ¹⁵N, and radiocarbon ages of soil organic carbon.
- **Key result:** HM sites had steeper SW slopes, lower mineralogical diversity, higher sand content, nutrient-poor soils, higher soil C:N, more depleted δ¹⁵N, and older soil organic carbon at 0–10 cm — all consistent with slowed SOM decomposition driven by reduced rhizosphere activity and suppressed priming.
- **Why it matters:** Dieback does not simply release carbon; it can lock carbon in slower-cycling pools. Models projecting soil C fate under subalpine conifer dieback must incorporate both site physicochemistry constraints and rhizosphere feedbacks, not just litter inputs.
- **Caveats:** Differences in volumetric soil water content between HM and LM were not statistically significant; causal direction of some feedbacks inferred from correlative biogeochemical signatures rather than manipulative experiments; single mountain site limits generalizability.
