# A global meta-analysis reveals that plastic mulch enhances crop yields at the cost of soil quality loss and nitrogen-cycle disruption driven by microplastic residues

**Authors:** Li X, Shaoyuan Feng, Xuewen Gong et al.
**Venue:** Field Crops Research · **Date:** 2026-04-18 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.fcr.2026.110499
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Plastic mulch raises crop yield +18.29% and WUE +33.67%, but accumulated microplastic residues degrade soil aggregation, suppress denitrification gene *nirS* by 85.79%, and reduce microbial diversity.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Plastic mulch is globally widespread (~30 Mha), but degrading films leave microplastic (MP) residues that may offset agronomic gains through soil quality and N-cycle disruption — prior studies were inconsistent and lacked a unified synthesis comparing PE film, biodegradable film, and MPs separately.
- **Method:** Global meta-analysis of 134 studies, 2229 data points; random-effects lnRR models; outcomes spanned crop growth traits, soil physicochemical properties (aggregation, porosity, DOC, TN, mineral N), enzyme activities (urease, phosphatase, catalase, C-enzymes), microbial biomass and diversity indices, and N-cycling functional genes (*nirS*, *nirK*, *nifH*, AOA, AOB).
- **Key result:** Plastic mulch: yield +18.29%, WUE +33.67%. MPs reduced yield by 72.29% overall (−84.10% at ≤1% w/w concentration). Soil aggregation −10.77%, porosity −6.48% under BF. MPs at >1% w/w: NH₄⁺-N +16.77%, NO₃⁻-N −27.75%. *nirS* suppressed −85.79% by MPs; AOB abundance −47.69%. Microbial biomass N +93.48% and C +21.02% at >1% MPs. Diversity: Shannon −1.59%, Simpson −12.54%, Chao1 −2.18%. Low-dose MPs (<1%): urease +3.46%, catalase +10.74%, C-enzymes +14.45%.
- **Why it matters:** Long-term plastic mulch converts a yield benefit into net soil degradation as MPs accumulate (~19 g/m² after 20 years); suppression of *nirS* denitrifiers and AOB indicates structural disruption of soil N cycling, not just transient chemical shifts. Biodegradable films reduce but do not eliminate the problem.
- **Caveats:** The 1% w/w MP threshold is a methodological classification, not an environmental benchmark. Most studies are short-term; long-term field trajectories of MP accumulation are underrepresented. Heterogeneity across soil types, climates, and crop species is high. Relative abundance data for microbial genes may not fully capture absolute functional change.
