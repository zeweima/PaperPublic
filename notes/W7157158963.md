# Reconciling soil organic carbon and crop productivity through agricultural practices in the drylands of Northwest China

**Authors:** Yafei Chen, Qiuyu Sun, Zhenzhen Song et al.
**Venue:** Geoderma · **Date:** 2026-04-28 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.geoderma.2026.117822
**Score:** 8/10 <sup>top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Mineral-organic fertilization achieves the strongest SOC-yield synergy (index 1.00) in Northwest China drylands; biochar input offers the broadest spatial applicability (96% mean synergy probability).

## Summary

- **Problem:** In arid Northwest China, SOC depletion and stagnant yields co-occur, yet trade-offs between carbon accumulation and nutrient supply make it unclear which agricultural practices (APs) can simultaneously improve both under chronic water limitation and alkaline, low-SOC soils.
- **Method:** Multilevel meta-analysis and random forest models on 5,245 paired observations from 267 publications; eight APs assessed (mineral fertilization, mineral-organic fertilization, biochar input, straw returning, green manure, no-till, subsoiling, mulch planting); novel synergy index quantifying co-enhancement of SOC and yield; binary random forest classifier predicting regional synergy probability mapped across Northwest China.
- **Key result:** Mineral-organic fertilization had the highest synergy index (1.00 vs. mineral fertilizer NF baseline), followed by biochar (0.31), straw returning (0.20), subsoiling (0.15), and green manure (0.15). No-till increased SOC 7.6% but had no significant effect on yield. Mulch planting raised yield 28.2% without significantly increasing SOC. Mineral-organic fertilization increased SOC 32.6% and yield 62.7% vs. no-fertilizer controls. Biochar showed the highest modeled synergy probability (96.0 ± 5.0%) across the region; green manure and no-till performed poorly in Xinjiang, western Inner Mongolia, and southern Gansu.
- **Why it matters:** Establishes that national or global AP recommendations cannot be directly extrapolated to arid Northwest China; spatially explicit maps identify where biochar input and mineral-organic fertilization are most promising, providing a policy-relevant framework for simultaneous food security and soil carbon goals in dryland agroecosystems.
- **Caveats:** SOC measured only in 0–20 cm layer; no SOC fraction or microbial process data; regional prediction maps are exploratory (coarse inputs, extrapolation uncertainty); heterogeneity in field experiments not fully controlled; subsoiling and no-till can cause vertical SOC stratification biasing cross-practice comparisons.
