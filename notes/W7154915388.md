# Reactive minerals and physicochemical protection drive soil organic carbon accumulation along a rainforest toposequence

**Authors:** Joshua T. Bennett-Jones, Peter M. Kopittke, Wenxiang Zhou et al.
**Venue:** Geoderma · **Date:** 2026 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.geoderma.2026.117820
**Score:** 8/10
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

SOC increases 77% with elevation in an Australian rainforest toposequence, driven by reactive Fe/Al-oxyhydroxide mineral formation and MAOM accumulation, not by molecular recalcitrance.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Mechanisms governing SOC persistence along environmental gradients are poorly constrained — specifically, whether accumulation reflects selective preservation of recalcitrant molecules, physicochemical mineral protection, or both.
- **Method:** 300–1100 m altitudinal toposequence in Lamington National Park (Gondwanan subtropical rainforest, Australia); 20 sites across 5 elevations. Density-based fractionation into fPOM, oPOM, and MAOM; bulk elemental analysis; XRD mineralogy; C K-edge NEXAFS spectroscopy on bulk soils and all fractions (100 samples total at Australian Synchrotron).
- **Key result:** Bulk SOC rose from 71 g kg⁻¹ (300 m) to 126 g kg⁻¹ (1100 m, +77%). MAOM-C increased from 26.5 to 73.2 g kg⁻¹ (+176%), reaching 81% of theoretical mineral saturation capacity at 1100 m. Fe- and Al-oxyhydroxides grew from 18% to 87% of total mineral content with elevation. Despite the large SOC increase, NEXAFS functional group composition remained constant across all elevations and fractions (<6% change), with carboxyl-C (~44%) and aliphatic-C (~26%) dominating throughout.
- **Why it matters:** Demonstrates that SOC accumulation is governed by mineral assemblage and physicochemical protection pathways (ligand exchange, co-precipitation, aggregate occlusion) rather than molecular recalcitrance or selective preservation; challenges models that attribute elevation SOC gradients primarily to POM accumulation or chemical stabilisation of specific functional groups. Effective saturation capacity averaged 51.6 g kg⁻¹ silt+clay, comparable to published global thresholds (41–54 g kg⁻¹).
- **Caveats:** Single toposequence on basalt/rhyolite parent material limits transferability; 1100 m site excluded from saturation analysis due to aggregate stability preventing adequate MAOM/cMAOM separation; climate and mineralogy co-vary along the gradient making causal attribution uncertain.
