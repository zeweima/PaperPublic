## TL;DR

First statistically validated remote sensing baseline for Congo Basin deforestation and degradation across six countries (2015–2023) shows stable deforestation rates but growing degradation, driven primarily by small-scale agriculture and forestry.

## Summary

- **Problem:** No systematic, statistically validated regional assessment of both deforestation and forest degradation existed for the six Congo Basin countries, leaving a gap in tracking forest change in Africa's largest contiguous tropical forest.
- **Method:** Satellite remote sensing analysis over 8 years (2015–2023) using open-source tools and sample-based area estimation across six Central African countries; identifies direct drivers of anthropogenic disturbance (small-scale vs. industrial).
- **Key result:** Deforestation across the six-country study region showed no significant increase over 2015–2023; degradation is more dynamic and increasing in some countries, potentially indicating opening of new deforestation fronts. Small-scale agriculture and forestry are the dominant drivers; industrial drivers are present but more limited in area, though likely more permanent in impact.
- **Why it matters:** Establishes the first regionally consistent, validated baseline for Congo Basin forest change monitoring, directly informing national REDD+ accounting and international climate commitments for a globally significant carbon sink.
- **Caveats:** Drivers and trends are aggregated at national/regional scale; sub-national hotspot dynamics may be obscured. Degradation detection from satellite data carries higher uncertainty than deforestation. No biomass flux quantification reported.
