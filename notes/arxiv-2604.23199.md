# How modeling assumptions shape predictions of convective mixing of carbon dioxide

**Authors:** Marco De Paoli, Sergio Pirozzoli
**Venue:** arXiv (physics.flu-dyn) · **Date:** 2026-04-25 · **DOI:** n/a
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR
Simplified CO₂ storage models (monotonic density laws, 2D flow, fixed interfaces) can mis-predict subsurface convective mixing rates by 10–100%.

## Summary
- **Problem:** Subsurface CO₂ storage security depends on convective mixing of dissolved CO₂ into brine, but most models use simplifying assumptions — monotonic density–concentration relationships, 2D flow, and fixed (non-deformable) CO₂–brine interfaces — whose combined error on predicted mixing rates was unquantified.
- **Method:** High-resolution Darcy-scale numerical simulations at Rayleigh-Darcy numbers O(10⁴), spanning 2D and 3D geometries, three density–concentration laws (linear, nonlinear-parabolic, non-monotonic), and fixed vs. free (deformable) interfaces. Mixing state tracked via mean scalar dissipation χ and a degree-of-mixing metric M(t); shutdown dynamics modelled with an analytical expression fitted to simulations (a₁ = 3.376, a₂ = 652.9).
- **Key result:** The shutdown regime drives 50–80% of total mixing. Assuming monotonic density laws or 2D flow introduces deviations of O(10–100)% in predicted mixing rates relative to the most complete (3D, non-monotonic, free-interface) model. Free interfaces accelerate early-time mixing through interfacial deformation and compression. Dimensionality causes up to 20% faster or 6% slower mixing in 3D vs. 2D depending on fluid parameters (α, β). The position of density maximum (parameter α) is the dominant fluid property: smaller α → faster mixing.
- **Why it matters:** Provides a unifying scalar-dissipation framework and quantifies the error budget of common modeling choices, directly guiding model selection for CO₂ storage site assessments and other subsurface reactive-transport problems.
- **Caveats:** Simulations are in confined, homogeneous porous media with constant viscosity and porosity; geological heterogeneity, capillary effects, and reactive geochemistry are excluded. Results apply at high Ra (O(10⁴)); behaviour at lower Ra relevant to low-permeability formations may differ.
