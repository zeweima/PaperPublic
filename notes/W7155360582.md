# Technical note: High Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiencies conceal poor simulations of interannual variance in seasonal regimes

**Authors:** Sacha W. Ruzzante, Wouter J. M. Knoben, Thorsten Wagener et al.
**Venue:** HESS · **Date:** 2026-04-23 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.5194/hess-30-2337-2026
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR
High NSE/KGE in seasonal catchments masks systematically poor interannual variance simulation across 18 hydrologic models, threatening climate-change predictions in tropical, alpine, and polar regions.

## Summary
- **Problem:** NSE and KGE aggregate seasonal, interannual, and irregular variance into one score; models that excel at reproducing the dominant seasonal cycle can achieve high scores while failing to capture year-to-year variability — exactly the component needed to project hydrologic change under climate change.
- **Method:** Fourier-based time series decomposition of streamflow into three orthogonal variance components (seasonal: >2 yr⁻¹; interannual: <2 yr⁻¹; irregular) applied to 17,245 gauges (28,406 compiled globally). Simulations from 18 regional and global models (6 LSTMs, 11 process-based, 1 hybrid) covering diverse climates were re-evaluated against component-specific NSE. Additionally, Spearman correlations for 41 ecologically relevant annual hydrologic signatures were compared between highly seasonal (σ²_seasonal/σ²_o > 0.5) and less seasonal catchments.
- **Key result:** All 18 models achieve significantly higher overall NSE in highly seasonal catchments (e.g., GLOB-LSTM1 median NSE = 0.74 vs. 0.54 in less seasonal), but NSE_interannual is significantly lower in highly seasonal catchments for 17 of 18 models (e.g., GLOB-LSTM1: 0.55 vs. 0.67). Interannual signature correlations are worse in highly seasonal catchments for 80% of model-metric combinations; for >50% of the 41 signatures, all models but one perform worse in highly seasonal catchments. Climatological benchmark NSE_cb exceeds 0.5 in 10% of gauges globally, concentrated in tropical monsoon and cold/polar climates — the same regions where interannual performance is worst.
- **Why it matters:** Using NSE/KGE thresholds uniformly across catchment types misleads model selection; models that appear best-in-class in tropical, alpine, and polar catchments may in fact have near-zero skill at simulating year-to-year variability, undermining climate-change impact assessments in regions most vulnerable to non-stationarity. The paper calls for interannual NSE and annual hydrologic signature correlations as explicit calibration targets.
- **Caveats:** Analysis does not identify mechanistic causes of poor interannual performance (model structure, forcing data quality, sparse gauge networks, and optimization algorithms are all hypothesized). Highly seasonal catchments are severely underrepresented in global benchmark datasets (e.g., tropical rainforest = 0.9% of CAMELS-type catchments vs. 6.2% of global land), so cross-model comparisons carry uncertainty. Six of 18 models were basin-calibrated, complicating direct comparison with globally calibrated ones.
