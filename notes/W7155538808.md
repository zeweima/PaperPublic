# Influence of Atmospheric Rivers on Lake Ice Phenology in the Northern Hemisphere

**Authors:** Yu Cai, Yao Xiao, Xiaoyi Shen et al.
**Venue:** Geophysical Research Letters · **Date:** 2026-04-24 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1029/2025gl120938
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR
Atmospheric rivers explain 20–33% of long-term lake ice variability across the Northern Hemisphere (1951–2022), primarily via +3.1°C temperature anomalies during AR events.

## Summary
- **Problem:** AR impacts on sea ice and glaciers are established, but no hemispheric-scale assessment existed for inland lake ice phenology — a key Essential Climate Variable tracking freeze–thaw cycles.
- **Method:** 652 Northern Hemisphere lakes with in situ ice phenology records (1951–2022) cross-referenced against the Global ARs Database v4 (ERA5-based, 0.25° resolution) and ERA5-Land climate variables (temperature, solar radiation, snowfall, rainfall). Multiple linear regression with dominance analysis quantified relative contributions of AR-associated climate anomalies; 103 lakes had full 72-year records for long-term analysis.
- **Key result:** AR events produced mean +3.1°C temperature anomalies (autumn–winter), +3.4 mm rainfall, +0.7 mm snowfall, and negative solar radiation anomalies. AR-associated climate anomalies explained on average R²=0.20 for ice-on, R²=0.33 for ice-off, and R²=0.30 for ice duration; North American lakes showed stronger responses (ice-off and duration R²=0.38) than European lakes (~0.20–0.25). Temperature anomalies contributed ~47–52% of total explanatory power; solar radiation ~22–26%; snowfall and rainfall 8–19%. Extreme short ice-duration years aligned closely with high AR frequency and intensity.
- **Why it matters:** ARs are an underappreciated driver of interannual lake ice variability; with AR frequency projected to increase under warming, ice duration shortening and extreme ice events will likely intensify, cascading into cold-region hydrology, ecosystems, and human infrastructure.
- **Caveats:** AR influence should be interpreted within the background of climate warming (detrending applied but ARs may act as a warming pathway, not independent driver); observation sites decline sharply after the 1990s, biasing toward North America and Europe; lake-specific factors (depth, morphology) modulating ice responses are unaccounted for; lagged AR effects and teleconnection interactions (ENSO, NAO) remain unexplored.
