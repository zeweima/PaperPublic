# [Evolutionary-based remodeling of ABA receptors reveals the structural basis of hormone perception and regulation](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2534140123)

**Authors:** María Rivera-Moreno, Mar Bono, Lourdes Infantes et al.
**Venue:** PNAS · **Date:** 2026-04-15 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2534140123
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

Monomeric high-affinity ABA receptors decode mild drought stress; dimeric low-affinity receptors extend signaling range under acute drought, resolving a long-standing apparent paradox.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Why do low-affinity dimeric ABA receptors exist as an evolutionary innovation if monomeric, high-affinity receptors already perceive the stress hormone effectively?
- **Method:** Evolutionary-based receptor remodeling of MpPYL1 (a liverwort ABA receptor); structural analyses identifying residues enforcing dimerization and lowering ABA affinity; in planta reporter assays and mutant analyses.
- **Key result:** Monomeric high-affinity receptors operate under low ABA concentrations (mild stress); dimeric reduced-affinity receptors sustain signaling at high ABA during acute drought — together expanding the system's dynamic range and robustness. Specific MpPYL1 residues enforce dimerization and reduce ABA affinity.
- **Why it matters:** Resolves the evolutionary paradox of dimeric low-affinity receptors; provides structural design principles for engineering ABA signaling pathways to enhance crop drought resilience.
- **Caveats:** Structural and in planta work based on MpPYL1 (liverwort); generalizability to crop ABA receptor families and quantitative ABA concentration thresholds not specified in abstract.
