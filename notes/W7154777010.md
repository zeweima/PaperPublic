# [Atmospheric CO₂ observations reveal seasonality bias in Arctic–boreal carbon flux estimates](https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/ae6128)

**Authors:** Jiaming Wen, Wu Sun, Kelsey T Foster et al.
**Venue:** Environmental Research Letters · **Date:** 2026-04-17 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/ae6128
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

Arctic–boreal NEE estimates from inversions, upscaled flux towers, and TBMs diverge most in seasonality, with GPP phenology errors — not respiration — as the dominant cause.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Net ecosystem exchange (NEE) in Arctic–boreal regions is estimated by three independent approaches (atmospheric inversions, upscaled eddy-covariance measurements, terrestrial biosphere models), but it is unclear how consistent these are with actual atmospheric CO₂ signals, and which process errors drive discrepancies.
- **Method:** Atmospheric CO₂ profiles from the CARVE (2012–2014) and Arctic-CAP (2017) airborne campaigns were used as observational benchmarks. Model–data consistency was assessed by comparing simulated vs. observed CO₂ variability across all three NEE estimate types.
- **Key result:** Model–data consistency is strongly tied to NEE seasonality; poorer-performing models show earlier spring onset, earlier uptake peak, or delayed autumn senescence. Seasonality biases stem primarily from gross primary production (GPP) errors and, secondarily, from heterotrophic respiration timing.
- **Why it matters:** Phenology errors in GPP propagate into large-scale carbon budget uncertainties for the rapidly warming Arctic–boreal zone; correcting these biases is prerequisite for reliable carbon–climate feedback projections.
- **Caveats:** Airborne campaigns span only 2012–2014 and 2017, limiting interannual inference; spatial coverage of CARVE/Arctic-CAP is not pan-Arctic. Process-level attribution relies on model decomposition rather than direct measurement of GPP and respiration.
