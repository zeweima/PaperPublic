# [Glacier Melt as a Source of Mercury: Implications for Ecosystem Recovery and Environmental Trends](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.est.5c16308)

**Authors:** Davide Mattio, Stéphane Guédron, Pierre Sabatier et al.
**Venue:** Environmental Science & Technology · **Date:** 2026-04-15 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.est.5c16308
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

Glacial meltwater in a proglacial Alpine lake shows still-rising Hg accumulation despite decades of declining regional atmospheric emissions, masking policy-driven recovery signals.

## Summary

- **Problem:** European Hg atmospheric emissions declined after mid-20th-century peak, but climate-driven remobilization from glaciers may offset expected ecosystem recovery — this confound is poorly documented at the lake-sediment level.
- **Method:** Paired sediment records from two neighboring high-altitude French Alpine lakes: Grand Lake (GDL, rain-fed, reflects regional atmospheric deposition) vs. Eychauda Lake (EYC, proglacial, receives glacial meltwater and erosion sediment). Short-lived radionuclide chronologies used to reconstruct ~100-year Hg deposition histories.
- **Key result:** GDL Hg accumulation tracks declining regional anthropogenic emissions as expected; EYC Hg accumulation rate has continued to increase in recent decades, correlated with erosion proxies — consistent with ice-stored Hg being released by accelerating glacial melt.
- **Why it matters:** Policy effectiveness assessments that rely on lacustrine sediment records from proglacial systems will be systematically misleading; glacier-derived Hg imposes a climate-change penalty that delays apparent — and likely actual — ecosystem recovery timelines.
- **Caveats:** Two-lake comparison limits spatial generalization; magnitude of glacier Hg stock and future release flux not directly quantified; other erosion-linked Hg sources (rock weathering) not fully ruled out.
