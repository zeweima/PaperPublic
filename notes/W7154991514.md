# Genotype × environment interaction and multi-trait stability of cotton lint yield and fiber quality across irrigated and rainfed environments

**Authors:** Mohan K. Bista, William Rutland, Brian Pieralisi et al.
**Venue:** Field Crops Research · **Date:** 2026 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.fcr.2026.110484
**Score:** 8/10
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Multi-trait stability index (MTSI) across 15 Mississippi environments identified DG 4530 B3TXF and DG 3528 B3XF as highest-productivity, highest-stability cultivars for both lint yield and fiber quality.

## Key result

- Environment dominated phenotypic variance for lint yield (88.5% E vs. 1.75% G), whereas genotypic variance was comparatively larger for fiber quality traits (up to 44.1% G for fiber strength).
- GEI explained 7.37–18.48% of total phenotypic variance; G:GEI ratio lower for lint yield (0.24) than for fiber quality traits (>1), indicating lint yield rankings are more environmentally labile than fiber quality rankings.
- Lint yield ranged 1324–1661 kg ha⁻¹ across genotypes; mean 1529 kg ha⁻¹ across all environments.
- GGE analysis identified four mega-environments for lint yield and two for Q-score; winner genotypes differed between MEs, confirming environment-specific adaptation.
- WAASBY selection differential for lint yield was 26.7% when jointly optimizing productivity and stability, vs. 3.4% for productivity alone — stability selection captures substantially more variation among commercial cultivars.
- At 15% selection intensity, MTSI selected G04 (DG 4530 B3TXF) and G03 (DG 3528 B3XF); MTSI scores 3.23 and 3.57 respectively.
- Lint yield showed only moderate cross-regime correlation (r = 0.51, p < 0.05) between irrigated and rainfed environments, while all fiber quality traits correlated strongly (r = 0.86–0.99, p < 0.001).

## Summary

- **Problem:** Cotton lint yield and fiber quality respond differently to irrigated vs. rainfed conditions; single-trait or single-environment selection misses GEI complexity and yield–quality trade-offs.
- **Method:** 16 upland cotton genotypes evaluated in 15 environments (5 irrigated, 10 rainfed) across Mississippi (2023–2024 seasons, MAFES variety trials). REML variance partitioning, AMMI, GGE biplots, WAASB/WAASBY stability indices, and MTSI applied to lint yield plus 6 fiber quality traits (fiber length, strength, micronaire, uniformity, color grade, Q-score).
- **Key result:** See above. Environment drove >88% of lint yield variance; genotype dominated fiber quality. Trade-off: increasing lint yield and fiber length came at cost of reduced fiber strength and micronaire. MTSI outperformed single-trait indices by capturing balanced productivity–stability across all traits simultaneously.
- **Why it matters:** Demonstrates that ignoring stability in cotton cultivar selection severely underestimates genotypic variation; MTSI framework practical for breeders targeting climate-variable production regions. Mega-environment boundaries did not align with irrigated/rainfed management zones, suggesting agroecological factors beyond water regime drive adaptation.
- **Caveats:** Two-year, single-state (Mississippi) dataset; 16 commercial cultivars only — limited genetic diversity. Seasonal weather variables explained GEI patterns weakly, leaving mega-environment drivers partly unresolved. Color grade heritability low (h² = 0.45); MTSI sensitive to ideotype trait weighting choices.
