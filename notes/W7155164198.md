# Transformed-stationary EVA 2.0: a generalized framework for non-stationary multivariate extremes analysis

**Authors:** Mohammad Hadi Bahmanpour, Alois Tilloy, Michalis Vousdoukas et al.
**Venue:** HESS (Hydrology and Earth System Sciences) · **Date:** 2026-04-22 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.5194/hess-30-2301-2026
**Score:** 9/10 <sup>top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Extending tsEVA to multivariate settings via time-dependent copulas captures non-stationary joint extremes that stationary and univariate approaches miss, with Spearman coupling between La Liane river discharge and wave height rising from ~0.1 in 1950 to >0.35 by 2020.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Non-stationary extreme value analysis is well developed for univariate series, but no general framework exists for compound (multi-variable) extremes whose marginal distributions and inter-variable dependence both evolve over time.
- **Method:** tsEVA 2.0 combines the transformed-stationary EVA approach (Mentaschi et al. 2016) with copula theory. Univariate marginals are stationarized via time-varying renormalization, fitted with GEV or GPD, and back-transformed. Joint extremes are sampled by block-maxima or POT methods; a time-varying copula (Gaussian, Gumbel, or Frank, fitted in a sliding 30–40-year window) models evolving dependence. Goodness-of-fit uses Cramér–von Mises statistics plus Kendall and Spearman correlation differences between data and Monte Carlo samples. Three case studies: (1) bivariate river discharge × significant wave height (SWH) at La Liane, France, 1950–2020; (2) trivariate SWH at three Marshall Island locations, ~1950–2024; (3) bivariate surface temperature × SPEI-6 near Milan, 1959–2023. Open-source MATLAB toolbox (tsEVA 2.0) released on GitHub.
- **Key result:** Case 1—Gumbel coupling parameter rose significantly (p = 4.7×10⁻⁵), Spearman ρ from <0.1 to >0.35 over 70 years; 10- and 50-year joint return-level curves shifted markedly between 1950–1990 and 1980–2020 windows. Case 2—Gumbel θ increased for all three location pairs (e.g., P1–P2: 1.98→2.46; P2–P3: 1.42→1.61), all trends significant (p < 0.05), indicating expanding spatial extent of extreme wave events. Case 3—No significant trend in coupling parameter (p = 0.102); non-stationarity in the temperature × drought joint distribution is driven by the marginals, not by changing dependence.
- **Why it matters:** Univariate or stationary compound-event frameworks systematically misrepresent joint return periods for natural hazard design; tsEVA 2.0 provides a diagnostic and estimation tool that can distinguish whether increasing compound risk stems from intensifying marginals, evolving dependence, or both.
- **Caveats:** Case studies rely on model-reanalysis output (HERA, global wave model) rather than dense observations, introducing potential bias; framework is currently MATLAB only (Python port in development); block-independence assumptions underlying GEV may be challenged for slowly-evolving variables like SPEI-6; computational cost of sliding-window copula fitting limits temporal resolution.
