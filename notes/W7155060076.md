# Atmospheric oxygen constraints on Southern Ocean productivity and drivers of carbon uptake

**Authors:** <unclear from abstract>

**Score:** 9/10
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

Airborne O₂/N₂ observations constrain Southern Ocean net primary production at 6.5 ± 1.36 PgC yr⁻¹ — higher than most CMIP6 and satellite estimates — and reduce end-of-century CO₂ uptake uncertainty by 53%.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Southern Ocean net primary production (NPP) is poorly constrained due to sparse observations; CMIP6 models and satellites disagree substantially, and the resulting uncertainty propagates into projections of Southern Ocean CO₂ uptake.
- **Method:** Linked CMIP6-modelled NPP to modelled air–sea O₂ fluxes, then applied O₂ flux estimates derived from airborne O₂/N₂ observations to constrain NPP south of ~44° S; benchmarks combined with airborne CO₂ and surface ocean pCO₂/temperature observations to reduce model spread.
- **Key result:** Annual Southern Ocean NPP = 6.5 ± 1.36 PgC yr⁻¹, higher than most CMIP6 and satellite-based estimates but consistent with Argo oxygen-based estimates; models with underestimated NPP show weak summer CO₂ uptake, and some produce summer outgassing (wrong sign) vs. observed summer uptake; applying the multi-constraint framework reduces end-of-century Southern Ocean CO₂ uptake uncertainty by 53%.
- **Why it matters:** Establishes quantitative NPP benchmarks that expose a systematic CMIP6 bias (likely from inadequate vertical mixing representation), and substantially narrows uncertainty in long-term Southern Ocean carbon sink projections.
- **Caveats:** Constraint relies on airborne O₂/N₂ campaign coverage, which may be spatially and seasonally limited; uncertainty in air–sea O₂ flux parameterisations propagates into the NPP estimate; model structural errors in mixing may not be fully separable from other biases.
