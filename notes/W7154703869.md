# [The influence of waves and bubbles on oxygen in the ocean interior](https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/ae612b)

**Authors:** Paridhi Rustogi, Laure Resplandy, Seth Bushinsky et al.
**Venue:** Environmental Research Letters · **Date:** 2026-04-17 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/ae612b
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

Wave-breaking bubble exchange is a first-order control on ocean oxygen, reducing model-observation bias by 70–90% and raising interior oxygen by 2–10 μmol kg⁻¹ globally.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Conventional wind-only gas transfer formulations systematically underestimate ocean oxygen by ignoring bubble-mediated exchange during wave breaking, producing oxygen undersaturation biases relative to Argo float observations.
- **Method:** Implemented a generalized wind-wave-bubble gas transfer formulation (accounting for diffusive flux through unbroken surface plus pressurized large/small bubble dissolution) in a global ocean circulation model, run over 1959–2020; compared against quality-controlled biogeochemical Argo float oxygen concentrations in mode and deep water mass formation regions.
- **Key result:** Wave-bubble formulation reduces model-observation mismatch by ~70–90% in key water mass formation regions, raises interior oxygen by +2–10 μmol kg⁻¹ across most water mass layers, and amplifies the climatological seasonal amplitude of global air-sea oxygen flux by ~30%.
- **Why it matters:** Bubble-driven oxygen injection is a global-scale process, not a local correction; omitting it biases ocean deoxygenation projections and deep-water ventilation estimates. Results call for updating standard gas transfer parameterizations in Earth system models.
- **Caveats:** Study is based on a single global circulation model; wave climatology and bubble spectra uncertainties are not fully characterized; results for CO₂ and other gases would need separate evaluation since bubble effects are gas-solubility-dependent.
