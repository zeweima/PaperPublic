# Defining metabolic niches for marine microbial heterotrophs

**Authors:** Ryan Reynolds, Anna C.B. Weiss, Chase C. James et al.
**Venue:** Science Advances · **Date:** 2026-04-22 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.adz0537
**Score:** 9/10 top pick
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Eight metabolic niches defined from genome-scale metabolic models of 3738 marine heterotrophs replace taxonomy as the unit for linking heterotrophic diversity to biogeochemical rates.

## Summary

- **Problem:** No tractable framework connects heterotrophic microbial diversity to carbon cycling rates the way phytoplankton functional types do for carbon fixation; copiotrophic/oligotrophic binary is too coarse to parameterize biogeochemical models.
- **Method:** CarveMe v1.5.1 built genome-scale metabolic models (GEMs) in ensemble mode (N = 60 per genome) for 3738 heterotrophic bacterial genomes from the Ocean Microbial Database (>80% completeness, <5% contamination); retained 1578 high-quality models (consensus score ≥ 0.8). Flux balance analysis (COBRApy) under replete and substrate-limited conditions quantified growth sensitivity to 11 compound classes. Self-organizing maps (SOM) with k-means clustering (k = 8) grouped genomes into metabolic clusters. Biogeographic validation used competitive recruitment across 1209 metagenomes and 16S ASV data from Atlantic (GA02) and Pacific (P16N/S) transects.
- **Key result:** Eight metabolic clusters emerged: 1 fast-growing generalist (Cluster 6; 79.5% fast growers, mean dCUB = −0.235, no limiting substrate class), 3 slow-growing specialist oligotrophic clusters (Clusters 1, 5, 8; sensitive to 2+ compound classes including carboxylic acids, amino acids, peptides), and 4 intermediate clusters each limited by a single substrate class (amino acids, carboxylic acids, carbohydrates, or B vitamins). GEM substrate predictions matched experimental culture data at 75.5% accuracy (87.4% precision, significantly above randomized bootstrap baseline). Cluster abundances partitioned into three oceanographic ecotypes: estuarine (Cluster 6 enriched), coastal (Clusters 2 and 4), and oligotrophic open ocean (Clusters 1, 5, 7, 8); cluster–growth-rate correlation R² = 0.82.
- **Why it matters:** Provides a metabolic-capability-based (not taxonomic) functional type system for marine heterotrophs that can directly parameterize growth rates and substrate preferences in large-scale biogeochemical models, enabling explicit representation of heterotrophic diversity in ecosystem simulations of carbon cycling.
- **Caveats:** CarveMe failed to generate high-quality models for many key groups (e.g., Pelagibacterales, Marinisomatales: only 10.8% and 23.3% of genomes reached consensus ≥ 0.8), so subsurface and oligotrophic communities are underrepresented. Clusters are built on genomic proxies; in situ metabolite availability and cross-feeding interactions are not captured. Annotation gaps in transporter genes are a likely driver of model failures.
