# Dynamic Parameterization of Priestley-Taylor Coefficient for a More Accurate Potential Evapotranspiration Estimation in Ungauged Regions

**Authors:** Pushpendra Raghav, Mukesh Kumar
**Venue:** Environmental Research Letters · **Date:** 2026-04-24 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/ae6464
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR
Biome-specific, dynamically estimated Priestley-Taylor alpha (driven by temperature and radiation) outperforms the fixed 1.26 for PET, ET, and plant stress across 246 FluxNet sites.

## Summary
- **Problem:** The Priestley-Taylor method fixes alpha_PT = 1.26 globally, ignoring biome and seasonal variability and degrading PET accuracy in ungauged regions lacking calibration data.
- **Method:** 3,128 site-years of eddy covariance data from 246 FluxNet sites worldwide used to optimize alpha_PT per biome; temperature and radiation identified as key drivers; functional equations derived to predict alpha_PT from bio-environmental variables without requiring flux measurements.
- **Key result:** alpha_PT shows significant spatial and seasonal variability — higher in forests and winter, lower in savannas in summer. Dynamic alpha applied to PET improved estimates of actual ET and the Evaporative Stress Index (ESI) relative to fixed-alpha baseline; specific RMSE/bias reductions not resolvable from available text.
- **Why it matters:** Drop-in functional equations enable more accurate PET in ungauged basins and can be embedded directly in land surface models (LSMs) and hydrologic models to improve drought assessment, water-balance closure, and remote-sensing-based ET products.
- **Caveats:** Quantitative improvement metrics (e.g., RMSE reduction, Nash-Sutcliffe gains) are not available from the abstract; FluxNet site distribution may under-represent tropical and arid regions; functional equations derived from observational biomes may not extrapolate well to novel climate states.
