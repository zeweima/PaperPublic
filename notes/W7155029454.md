# Beyond the Nutrient Paradigm: Thermal Intensity and Duration Trigger Opposite Phytoplankton Responses to Marine Heatwaves in the East China Sea

**Authors:** Jiarui Zhou, Xin Liu, Edward A. Laws et al.
**Venue:** Geophysical Research Letters · **Date:** 2026 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1029/2025gl121124
**Score:** 8/10
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Short-intense marine heatwaves stimulate phytoplankton in nutrient-rich waters while long-weak events suppress it, with duration increasing 62% globally vs. only 5.5% intensity increase since the early record.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Marine heatwave (MHW) impacts on phytoplankton are typically framed around nutrient limitation alone; direct thermal effects of MHW intensity vs. duration are poorly constrained from observational data.
- **Method:** Satellite Chl-a (ESA OC-CCI, 4-km, 1998–2024) and NOAA OISST validated against 1,287 in situ measurements from 65 cruises in the East China Sea; background nitrate from WOA2023; MHW events classified as short-intense (MHW_si: duration <15 days, mean intensity >1.67°C) vs. long-weak (MHW_lw: duration ≥15 days, mean intensity ≤1.67°C); surface mixed-layer heat-budget framework to diagnose atmospheric vs. ocean-driven events; global comparison in supporting figures.
- **Key result:** MHW_si events produce positive Chl-a anomalies at background nitrate ≥2.80 mmol m⁻³; MHW_lw events require ≥7.26 mmol m⁻³ to turn positive and deliver mean Chl-a anomalies of −68% vs. −43% for MHW_si. At equal cumulative thermal intensity, rapid heating boosts Chl-a more than gradual warming but only in nutrient-rich waters (R² = 0.58, p < 0.001 for eutrophic duration–response; relationship absent in oligotrophic waters). In the Kuroshio region, spring Chl-a declines ~30% during MHWs. East China Sea MHW duration has increased 62% (7.01 to 11.34 days) over the last two decades while mean intensity rose only 5.5% (1.62°C to 1.71°C).
- **Why it matters:** Because MHW duration is growing faster than intensity under global warming, long-weak events will become more frequent, amplifying negative phytoplankton responses and cascading impacts on marine food webs and ocean carbon uptake; ecosystem models need to incorporate thermal rate-of-change effects alongside nutrient controls.
- **Caveats:** Causal disentanglement between thermal and nutrient pathways is not fully achieved with observational data; satellite Chl-a misses subsurface responses; nitrate thresholds derived for the East China Sea may not transfer to other regions; analysis uses a fixed early-period SST baseline which may inflate MHW detection under long-term warming trend.
