# [Restoring organic soils under agriculture: cost-effective portfolios in the context of European climate and biodiversity policies](https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/ae5fae)

**Authors:** Fanqi Jia, Андре Депперманн, Juraj Balkovič et al.
**Venue:** Environmental Research Letters · **Date:** 2026-04-15 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/ae5fae
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

EU drained agricultural organic soils could mitigate 38–44 MtCO₂e yr⁻¹ by 2050 via rewetting, with paludiculture enabling 2 Mha restoration even without carbon pricing.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Drained agricultural organic soils account for ~80% of EU cropland and grassland GHG emissions, yet cost-effectiveness of restoration measures and their policy synergies are poorly characterized.
- **Method:** EU-wide economic land-use model (GLOBIOM-EU) evaluated three restoration measures—full rewetting, rehabilitation, and full rewetting with paludiculture—through 2050; marginal abatement cost curves (MACCs) constructed across a range of GHG prices.
- **Key result:** At 100 EUR tCO₂e⁻¹, 38.2–44.4 MtCO₂e yr⁻¹ mitigated by 2050. Paludiculture product demand enables up to 2 Mha restoration without additional carbon price incentives, delivering 17 MtCO₂e yr⁻¹. Meeting the EU Nature Restoration Regulation (NRR) 2050 targets alone mitigates 20–26 MtCO₂e yr⁻¹ (23–29% of current emissions from drained organic soils).
- **Why it matters:** Paludiculture creates a market-driven pathway to large-scale rewetting independent of carbon policy; NRR biodiversity targets deliver substantial climate co-benefits at no additional climate-specific cost, supporting integrated land-use policy.
- **Caveats:** GLOBIOM-EU is an economic equilibrium model; biophysical GHG emission factors for rewetted peatlands carry substantial spatial uncertainty. Paludiculture market development is assumed but not guaranteed. Results are EU-specific and may not transfer to other peatland regions.
