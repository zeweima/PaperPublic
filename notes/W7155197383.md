# Agricultural drought stress forecasting across different biomes in Brazil using machine learning

**Authors:** Joseph William Gallear, Alice Milne, Stephan Haefele et al.
**Venue:** Agricultural and Forest Meteorology · **Date:** 2026-04-22 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.agrformet.2026.111132
**Score:** 9/10 <sup>top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Random forest models forecast agricultural drought stress (VHI < 40%) 1 month ahead across all six Brazilian biomes with accuracy, precision, and recall generally >0.8, but the Caatinga requires separate treatment due to its unique climate drivers.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Brazil lacks biome-specific, operationally useful 1-month-ahead forecasts of agricultural drought stress; it is unclear how meteorological drought propagates to crop-relevant vegetation stress differently across the country's six biomes.
- **Method:** Random forest classifiers trained separately per biome on ERA5, CHIRPS, NASA GRACE (RZSM), SPEI, and NOAA STAR VHI at 0.25° grid cells (Feb 2003–Dec 2021); binary target = monthly VHI < 40% threshold; leave-one-year-out cross-validation; Shapley (SHAP) values and EOF analysis for interpretability.
- **Key result:** Accuracy, precision, and recall exceed 0.8 for all biomes. Caatinga has the highest drought-to-non-drought ratio (0.48) and the highest true positive rate at the 40% VHI threshold (~0.8 vs ~0.4–0.5 for other biomes). Lagged VHI is by far the dominant predictor (Shapley contribution 1.4–3× other variables), reflecting slow inertia of vegetation health. Precipitation and SPEI rank in the top four features for most biomes. RZSM shows a strong spatial correlation with drought probability only in the Caatinga (r = −0.620), not in other biomes. EOF3 of RZSM/precipitation/VHI isolates a Northeast Brazil mode distinct from the rest of the country.
- **Why it matters:** Provides the first multi-biome operational drought forecast framework for Brazil at grid scale; demonstrates that Northeast Brazil (Caatinga) must be modelled separately; findings support integration with CEMADEN's integrated drought index. Strong Cerrado forecasts are especially economically relevant given Brazil's soybean export dependence.
- **Caveats:** Models use only climate inputs—no crop type, land cover, or management data—limiting specificity. Caatinga shows higher spatial recall variability and some drought overestimation in central areas. SHAP values can be sensitive to correlated features; authors caution against fine-scale interpretation without further sensitivity analysis. Increasing aridity trends in the Caatinga (post-study period) may degrade forecast calibration.
