# An unrecognized mode of small particles in the lower stratosphere

**Authors:** Ming Lyu, Adam T. Ahern, Gregory P. Schill et al.
**Venue:** Science · **Date:** 2026-04-23 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1126/science.adw8939
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

A previously undetected organic-rich fine-particle mode (~0.03–0.11 µm) dominates lower-stratosphere surface area and reactive-vapor sinks, invisible to satellites and current models.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Lower-stratospheric aerosol composition and size distribution are incompletely characterized; existing satellite and most balloon-borne optical instruments may miss a significant particle population with implications for heterogeneous chemistry and climate interventions.
- **Method:** In-situ measurements detecting particles at water vapor conditions >270 ppbv; size distribution characterized by number geometric mean diameter of ~0.03–0.11 µm (surface area 0.08–0.2 µm; volume 0.11–0.3 µm); compared against a global chemistry-climate model.
- **Key result:** Organic-rich particles transported from the troposphere form a distinct small-particle mode that dominates heterogeneous-reaction surface area and condensable-vapor sink; particles grow and decrease in number concentration as they mix with older stratospheric air; global chemistry-climate model fails to replicate mode characteristics.
- **Why it matters:** Current geoengineering assessments (e.g., stratospheric aerosol injection) rely on models that miss this particle class, potentially misrepresenting aerosol surface area, chemistry, and radiative effects; satellite retrievals also blind to it.
