# Temperature sensitivity of labile and stabilised organic carbon decomposition in different land uses

**Authors:** Tibor Filep, Dóra Zacháry, Marianna Ringer et al.
**Venue:** Biogeochemistry · **Date:** 2026-04-28 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10533-026-01338-8
**Score:** 9/10 top pick
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

Q₁₀ values (2.96–3.71) do not differ between labile and stabilised soil C fractions, but cultivated aggregate fractions show anomalously high Q₁₀ (5.38), driven by OM chemistry not pool lability.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Whether temperature sensitivity of decomposition (Q₁₀) differs between labile vs. stabilised organic C pools — a critical assumption in soil C models — remains unresolved and under-tested across land uses.
- **Method:** 1-year laboratory incubation of three soil fractions (POM = labile; sand+aggregate = aggregate-stabilised; silt+clay = mineral-associated) from four land uses (grass, spruce, oak, arable) at 15, 25, and 35 °C; Q₁₀ and decomposition rate constant (k) calculated per fraction.
- **Key result:** Decomposition rate k in POM exceeded stabilised fractions by 23–167%, but Q₁₀ did not differ significantly across fractions (range 2.96–3.71). Exception: arable soil aggregate fraction Q₁₀ = 5.38. Aryl C, methoxy C, and carbonyl C positively correlated with Q₁₀; O-alkyl C and C/N ratio negatively correlated.
- **Why it matters:** Refutes the kinetic theory expectation that recalcitrant (stabilised) pools should have higher Q₁₀; shows OM chemical composition — not pool label — governs temperature sensitivity. Highlights elevated vulnerability of aggregate-bound C in cultivated soils to warming.
- **Caveats:** Single site per land use; lab incubation may not capture field moisture and priming dynamics; 1-year window may not fully characterise slow pools.
