# Temperature sensitivity of labile and stabilised organic carbon decomposition in different land uses

**Authors:** Tibor Filep, Dóra Zacháry, Marianna Ringer et al.
**Venue:** Biogeochemistry · **Date:** 2026-04-28 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10533-026-01338-8
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

Q₁₀ of soil C decomposition is controlled by organic matter chemistry, not pool lability — aggregate-protected C in cultivated soils shows anomalously high temperature sensitivity (Q₁₀ = 5.38).

## Summary

- **Problem:** Whether labile vs. stabilised soil organic C pools differ in temperature sensitivity (Q₁₀) is unresolved, yet is critical for soil C turnover models and climate-feedback projections.
- **Method:** 1-year incubation at 15, 25, and 35 °C of three density/size fractions — particulate OM (POM; labile), sand+aggregate (S+A; aggregate-stabilised), silt+clay (s+c; mineral-associated OM) — from four land uses (grass, spruce, oak, arable). Decomposition rate k and Q₁₀ derived from CO₂ respiration.
- **Key result:** POM decomposition rate k exceeded stabilised fractions by 23–167%, but Q₁₀ did not differ significantly across pools (range 2.96–3.71), contradicting kinetic theory. Exception: arable aggregate fraction showed Q₁₀ = 5.38. OM chemistry drove Q₁₀: aryl C, methoxy C, carbonyl C correlated positively with Q₁₀; O-alkyl C and C/N ratio correlated negatively.
- **Why it matters:** Models that assign lower Q₁₀ to stable C pools may underestimate warming feedback in cultivated soils. Aggregate-protected C in croplands appears particularly vulnerable under warming, not just to physical disturbance.
- **Caveats:** Single-year lab incubation; four land-use sites; results may not transfer to waterlogged or calcareous soils. Mechanism linking OM chemistry to Q₁₀ is correlative, not causal.
