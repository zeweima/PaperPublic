# Effect of separation distance between on-site sanitation systems and groundwater sources on faecal contamination: A systematic review and meta-analysis

**Authors:** Gita L. Putri, Elzavira Felaza, Cindy R. Priadi et al.
**Venue:** Water Research · **Date:** 2026-04-20 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.watres.2026.125970
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Greater horizontal separation from on-site sanitation systems reduces groundwater faecal indicator bacteria ~92% per 10 m, but high heterogeneity (I² = 93%) means no single setback distance works universally.

## Summary

- **Problem:** 3.8 billion people rely on on-site sanitation (OSS) systems; setback distance standards exist but lack quantitative synthesis of how horizontal and vertical separation affect groundwater faecal indicator bacteria (FIB) levels.
- **Method:** PRISMA 2020 systematic review + random-effects meta-analyses of 59 studies (1900–September 2025) reporting FIB (E. coli or TTC) concentrations and OSS–groundwater distances; four analysis models (binary/continuous distance × binary/continuous FIB) tested for horizontal distance; moderating factors (OSS type, groundwater source, season, setting, flow type) examined via stratification and meta-regression. Within-study slopes (log₁₀ FIB per 10 m) estimated for 35 studies with ≥3 distance points.
- **Key result:** Pooled horizontal distance effect (continuous distance–continuous FIB): Fisher's z = −0.25 (95% CI: −0.40, −0.10; I² = 93%), explaining ~6% of FIB variance. FIB detection odds ~67% lower at >10 m vs ≤10 m (pooled logOR = −1.10; 95% CI: −1.64, −0.55). Within-study analysis: median slope = −1.2 log₁₀ per 10 m (~92% attenuation per 10 m). Piezometers showed stronger protective gradient than dug wells or boreholes. Vertical distance evidence inconclusive (only 2 eligible studies; CIs crossed zero in all models).
- **Why it matters:** Horizontal setback distance is a risk gradient, not a threshold—protection varies strongly by hydrogeological context, OSS type, and groundwater source. A single generic setback standard is unlikely to provide equivalent protection across settings, particularly in low- and middle-income countries where most included studies (51%) were conducted.
- **Caveats:** Very high heterogeneity (I² = 93%) limits generalizability of the pooled effect; only 14% of studies met statistical analysis quality criteria; vertical distance severely under-studied; bacterial FIB may not represent viral or protozoan risk (viruses travel farther, are not measured by FIB); outcome definition inconsistency (log₁₀-transformed vs. raw) adds variance.
