# Ocean stratification and tides control basal melting at the Ross Ice Shelf Grounding Zone

**Authors:** Craig Stevens, Craig L. Stewart, Natalie Robinson et al.
**Venue:** Science Advances · **Date:** 2026-04-24 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.ady8474
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR
A 10-month borehole record at the Kamb Ice Stream grounding zone shows tidal forcing at the spring-neap cycle governs basal melt rate, which averages 0.18 ± 0.02 m a⁻¹.

## Summary
- **Problem:** Ice shelf basal melt at grounding zones is poorly constrained because direct ocean observations are rare; existing models lack tidal and stratification processes operating at sub-seasonal timescales critical for projections.
- **Method:** Hot-water borehole drilled through 587 m of ice at KIS1, 4 km offshore the Kamb Ice Stream grounding line, Ross Ice Shelf. A 10-month mooring (Dec 2019–Sep 2020) with MicroCat CTDs, Aquadopp current meters, and SoundNine thermistors monitored a 30-m water column at ~30-min intervals; 84 CTD yo-yo profiles and ROV *Icefin* surveys provided spatial context.
- **Key result:** The water column is persistently two-layered — a warmer, saltier lower inflow layer (Ross Sea High Salinity Shelf Water, Θ ≈ −1.9°C) separated by a sharp pycnocline from a colder, fresher upper meltwater-laden outflow layer. Spring-neap tidal forcing dominates melt variability: melt during the 7-day spring-tide half of each 14-day cycle is ~3× that of the neap half. Parameterized mean melt rate is 0.18 ± 0.02 m a⁻¹ (drag coefficient Cd = 0.0036). Bulk Richardson number averages Ri_b = 0.52 but falls below the instability threshold (0.25) for 30% of the time, indicating frequent shear-driven interfacial mixing. Internal waves with ~6-m peak-to-peak pycnocline displacement are tidally generated and drive this episodic mixing.
- **Why it matters:** Demonstrates the grounding zone is a mechanistically distinct sub-region of the ice-shelf cavity where tidal baroclinicity and stratification jointly set basal melt, not captured in current CMIP6-scale models. Spring-neap melt pulses will imprint a ~14-day signal on cavity-wide thermohaline circulation and must be parameterized for credible sea-level projections.
- **Caveats:** Single borehole at a stagnant ice stream — conditions at dynamic or warm-water-accessed grounding zones (e.g., Thwaites) may differ substantially. Drag coefficient uncertainty propagates ~25% uncertainty into melt estimates. Lower altimeter was damaged on deployment, limiting direct tidal excursion data. Bathymetric heterogeneity and 3-D flow obliquity complicate extrapolation to broader grounding-zone scales.
