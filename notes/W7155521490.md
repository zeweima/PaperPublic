# Ocean stratification and tides control basal melting at the Ross Ice Shelf Grounding Zone

**Authors:** Craig Stevens, Craig L. Stewart, Natalie Robinson et al.
**Venue:** Science Advances · **Date:** 2026-04-24 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.ady8474
**Score:** 9/10 <sup>top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

A 10-month borehole record at the Kamb Ice Stream grounding zone shows tidal forcing at spring-neap timescales drives ~14-day cyclicity in basal melt, with melting ~10× greater during spring tides than neap.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Antarctic ice shelf grounding zones are nearly unobserved; the ocean processes governing basal melt initiation and boundary layer formation there are unknown, yet they set conditions for the entire sub-shelf cavity.
- **Method:** Hot-water borehole (587 m of ice) at the Kamb Ice Stream grounding zone (KIS1), Ross Ice Shelf. Nine CTD profile sequences plus a 10-month moored timeseries (5 thermistors, 2 Aquadopp current meters, 2 MicroCat CTDs) spanning December 2019–September 2020; ROV *Icefin* spatial surveys of seabed and ice base; CATS2008 tidal model comparison; bulk Richardson number and melt-rate parameterizations.
- **Key result:** A persistently stratified 30-m water column separates warm inflowing Ross Sea High Salinity Shelf Water (Θ ≈ −1.9°C, S_A ≈ 34.925 g kg⁻¹) below from a colder, fresher meltwater-laden upper layer. Mean basal melt rate estimated at 0.18 ± 0.025 m a⁻¹ (drag coefficient C_d = 0.0036). Spring-neap tidal forcing dominates all observed variability: total melt over the 7-day spring-tide period is ~3× that of the surrounding 3.5-day neap periods. Bulk Richardson number averages 0.52, with 30% of the time below the critical value (0.25), indicating frequent shear-driven interfacial mixing. Pycnocline vertical excursions reach 6–8 m within a 30-m column, confirming baroclinic internal wave activity.
- **Why it matters:** Grounding-zone stratification is mechanistically distinct from the central cavity; tidal mixing here modulates the meltwater flux that buoys the entire sub-shelf boundary layer. Climate projections relying on barotropic tidal models will miss the baroclinic mixing that controls near-grounding-line melt. The region functions analogously to a tidal estuary, with Ekman dynamics in the upper layer and spring-neap-phased melt pulses.
- **Caveats:** Single borehole location on a stagnant ice stream (Kamb); drag coefficient uncertainty propagates ~25% uncertainty in melt rate. Bathymetry beneath the shelf is poorly constrained, limiting tidal model skill especially around equinoxes. Results may not generalize to dynamically active ice streams with different basal roughness or water mass access.
