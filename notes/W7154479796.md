# [Observational constraints project a ~50% AMOC weakening by the end of this century](https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.adx4298)

**Authors:** Valentin Portmann, Didier Swingedouw, Omar Khattab, Marie Chavent
**Venue:** Science Advances · **Date:** 2026-04-15 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.adx4298
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Ridge-regularized regression on 19 observed SST/SSS variables constrains CMIP6 AMOC decline to 51 ± 8% by 2100, ~60% stronger than the raw multimodel mean.

## Summary

- **Problem:** CMIP6 models disagree widely on AMOC fate (32 ± 37% weakening under SSP2-4.5), with model uncertainty dominating (78% of total variance by 2100); unconstrained projections are too uncertain to guide adaptation.
- **Method:** Four observational constraint (OC) methods compared on 32 CMIP6 models using leave-one-out cross-validation: weighted average, random forest, unregularized linear regression, and ridge-regularized linear regression. Univariate case uses past AMOC (RAPID, 2005–2023 mean); multivariate case uses 19 variables — past AMOC plus SST and SSS averaged over nine Atlantic/Pacific regions (1900–2020 means). Ridge regression selected as best performer.
- **Key result:** Ridge-regularized multivariate OC constrains SSP2-4.5 AMOC weakening to 51 ± 8% (90% probability) vs. 32 ± 37% unconstrained, reducing model uncertainty by 79%. The correction is dominated by South Atlantic SSS bias (47% of total correction) and North Atlantic SST cold bias (36%), both linked to underestimated salt-advection feedback. Constrained estimates are consistent across SSP3-7.0 (48 ± 5%) and SSP5-8.5 (58 ± 5%).
- **Why it matters:** A >50% AMOC slowdown crosses the IPCC threshold for "substantial weakening," implying stronger ITCZ shifts, Sahel drying, and sea-level rise along North American/European coasts than current IPCC assessments convey; adaptation strategies calibrated to the MMM are likely underdesigned.
- **Caveats:** OC relies on the assumption that CMIP6 models correctly capture inter-model X–Y relationships; multimodel bias in South Atlantic SSS and North Atlantic SST is identified as a key driver but the physical mechanism is inferred, not proven. Results degrade when using trend-based rather than mean-state observable definitions, suggesting the anthropogenic signal in SST/SSS trends is not yet robustly emergent. Only 32 models available limits statistical power for multivariate regression.
