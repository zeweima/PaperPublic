# [Long-term soil carbon accumulation and nutrient depletion under intensive chemical fertilization in tropical rice systems](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.fcr.2026.110490)

**Authors:** Tomohiro Nishigaki, Miwa Arai, Takanori Okamoto et al.
**Venue:** Field Crops Research · **Date:** 2026-04-16 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.fcr.2026.110490
**Score:** <unclear from abstract>/10
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Decades of continuous flooded rice cropping accumulate soil C and N (0.017–0.024% yr⁻¹ TC) yet simultaneously halve indigenous N-supplying capacity, while K is mined below deficiency thresholds.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Whether long-term SOM accumulation under intensive irrigated rice improves or degrades soil fertility is unclear; existing evidence rarely separates SOM quantity from quality or tracks all major nutrient pools simultaneously over multi-decadal timescales.
- **Method:** Archived 0–15 cm soil samples from two Philippine long-term field experiments — LTCCE (continuous triple-rice, clayey Aquandic Epiaquoll, 1985–2019) and LTFE (double-rice, silty Entic Pellustert, 1985–2013) — analyzed for TC, TN, available N (AN), available P (AP), and exchangeable K (Ex-K); PCA and regression linked soil properties to grain yield trends across N and NPK fertilizer treatments.
- **Key result:** TC and TN rose steadily at 0.017–0.024% yr⁻¹ in the LTCCE (higher in high-N treatments) despite complete straw removal. Yet AN declined across all treatments, halving the AN/TN ratio to 0.052 by 2019 — indicating progressive accumulation of recalcitrant, N-immobilizing organic fractions. AP accumulated when fertilizer P inputs exceeded crop uptake but stabilized or declined when nutrient balances shifted; partial reversibility confirmed. Ex-K declined continuously in the LTCCE, falling below the deficiency threshold of 0.2 cmol_c kg⁻¹ in high-N plots (N3, N4) by 2019, with annual K removal estimated at 122–308 kg ha⁻¹. PCA showed grain yield was driven by short-term nutrient availability (PC2: AN, AP, Ex-K) rather than long-term SOM stocks (PC1) in the LTCCE; the reverse held in the LTFE where overall SOM status dominated yield.
- **Why it matters:** Demonstrates a soil fertility paradox — quantitative SOC gain coexists with qualitative N degradation — implying current high yields in continuous triple-rice systems are sustained by external inputs masking declining indigenous soil N supply. Informs management redesign: periodic soil aeration (AWD), strategic P drawdown, and reinforced K fertilization are needed to sustain productivity.
- **Caveats:** AN estimated via water-extractable organic C proxy due to limited archived sample volume; analysis confined to 0–15 cm puddled layer, missing deeper nutrient redistribution; LTFE terminated 2013, limiting comparison across periods; logistic K depletion model assumes zero lower asymptote, potentially underestimating residual mineral-derived K.
