# Hydraulic Effects of Channel Realignment and Floodplain Reconnection in a Headwater Stream

**Authors:** MT Perks, N. Barber, G. L. Heritage et al.
**Venue:** Water Resources Research · **Date:** 2026-04-28 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1029/2025wr041858
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Re-meandering 1 km of an engineered UK headwater stream doubled median flood-wave travel time but provided negligible peak-flow attenuation during large events.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Channel realignment and floodplain reconnection are increasingly deployed as nature-based flood solutions, but empirical evidence of their hydraulic effects in moderate-gradient, small-order headwater channels is sparse.
- **Method:** Pre/post monitoring (2018–2023) at Goldrill Beck, Cumbria, UK (25.45 km² catchment) paired with 2D hydraulic modeling (LISFLOOD-FP, 1 m resolution). 1 km of embanked single-thread channel restored to multi-thread, anastomosing form with embankments removed. 228 storm events analysed; model calibrated to Nash-Sutcliffe = 0.99 and validated against UAV-derived inundation extents.
- **Key result:** Median flood-wave transmission time increased from 15 to 40 min; median celerity fell from 1.50 to 0.61 m s⁻¹. Wetted area increased 47%. Hysteresis index shifted from mean −0.06 (pre) to −0.15 (post), indicating greater floodplain storage under low-to-intermediate flows (up to ~20 m³ s⁻¹). Modeled peak discharge reduction was ≤1% for low-intermediate events (Sim A) and peak discharge was slightly elevated for the large event (Sim B, AEP 0.02); floodplain roughness increase (grassland → woodland, Manning's n 0.06 → 0.15 m⁻¹/³) produced only marginal additional attenuation.
- **Why it matters:** Quantifies the ceiling on headwater re-meandering as a flood mitigation tool: transmission delay is real and meaningful, but peak attenuation is negligible for large, rarer events. Valley morphometry and channel gradient (~0.004 m m⁻¹) constrain kinematic wave dominance (T₊ > 79), limiting dynamic attenuation. Habitat diversity improved alongside the marginal hydraulic gains.
- **Caveats:** Single 1-km reach; moderate gradient site may not generalise to steeper or flatter systems. Rating curve extrapolation introduces uncertainty at high flows. Post-realignment period only ~2 years; channel self-adjustment ongoing. Bypass flows at the lower monitoring station complicate large-event analysis.
