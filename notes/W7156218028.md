# Methane leakage thresholds for net climate benefits of wastewater biogas recovery

**Authors:** Xiatong Li, Jun-Jie Zhu, Yuqing Yan et al.
**Venue:** Nature Sustainability · **Date:** 2026-04-27 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41893-026-01818-7
**Score:** 9/10 <sup>top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

US wastewater biogas systems lose net climate benefit once CH₄ leakage exceeds 2–10% of biogas produced, and a large fraction of current facilities already surpass that threshold.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Biogas recovery at water resource recovery facilities (WRRFs) is assumed to reduce GHG emissions, but unquantified methane leakage may negate or reverse that benefit; no national-scale leakage threshold analysis existed.
- **Method:** Compiled measured CH₄ leakage data from >50 US facilities plus all available literature; performed regional- and technology-specific scenario and sensitivity analyses covering heat recovery, grid emissions intensity, and biogas utilization pathways (combined heat & power vs. renewable natural gas).
- **Key result:** Observed leakage rates span 0.4–65%; a large fraction of facilities already exceed the net-zero emission threshold. Net-zero leakage thresholds range from 2% to 10% of biogas produced depending on technology and grid carbon intensity. Under a decarbonizing grid, renewable natural gas pathways become more viable than CHP.
- **Why it matters:** Provides a quantitative roadmap for converting most WRRFs from net climate liabilities to assets via leak detection and repair; makes the case for binding methane monitoring and mitigation policy at wastewater utilities.
- **Caveats:** Leakage dataset is heterogeneous and facility-sparse; threshold ranges are scenario-dependent; grid decarbonization trajectories assumed rather than measured.
