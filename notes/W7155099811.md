# Interactive effects of heat and drought on wheat yield change from synergistic to antagonistic as their severity increases

**Authors:** Arisede Chisaka, John P. Hammond, Shinichi Nakagawa et al.
**Venue:** Agricultural and Forest Meteorology · **Date:** 2026 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.agrformet.2026.111189
**Score:** 8/10
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

Combined drought and heat cause 49% wheat yield loss on average, but shift from synergistic to antagonistic as individual stress severity exceeds ~37% combined loss.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Whether co-occurring drought and heat stress on wheat yield interact synergistically, additively, or antagonistically is unresolved, hampering climate impact projections that typically treat stressors independently.
- **Method:** Multilevel meta-analysis of 141 fully factorial (2×2) drought × heat experiments from 36 publications (458 effect sizes, 1984–2023); log response ratio (lnRR) and standardized mean difference (SMD) effect sizes; meta-regression using expected yield loss (sum of independent stressor effects) as predictor of observed combined-stress yield loss.
- **Key result:** Combined drought + heat (DH) caused mean 49% yield loss vs. 27% (drought alone) and 22% (heat alone); on average interaction was additive (no significant departure from D + H sum). However, interaction type was strongly severity-dependent: when summed independent losses were below ~37%, stresses acted synergistically (DH > D + H); above that threshold they acted antagonistically (DH < D + H). Meta-regression slope = 0.55 (CI 0.52–0.58), intercept = −16.7%, significantly different from 1:1. Grain yield was most sensitive (losses of 31%, 36%, 59% under D, H, DH respectively); 1000 kernel weight, harvest index, and spike attributes responded similarly across stresses. *T. aestivum* showed greater heat losses (28%) than *T. turgidum* ssp. *durum* (22%).
- **Why it matters:** Climate projections using only independent stressor impacts will overestimate combined losses at high severity (antagonistic regime) and underestimate them at low severity (synergistic regime); site-specific adaptation planning requires accounting for local stress severity, and simple single-stressor experiments can inform combined-stress predictions via the meta-regression model.
- **Caveats:** Most studies used pot experiments (which exaggerate drought severity) and lab-imposed heat via late sowing or growth chambers, limiting realism; most pre-determined fixed soil water levels rather than progressive drought; elevated CO₂ interactions not captured; genotypic variation could not be robustly quantified; threshold of ~37% varied 32–44% across sensitivity analyses.
