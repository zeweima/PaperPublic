## Top picks

- [ ] **[A Robust Calibration and Evaluation Framework for Dynamic Catchment Characteristics in Hydrological Modeling](https://doi.org/10.5194/hess-30-2455-2026)** · Tian Lan et al. · *HESS* · [notes](../notes/W7158965994.md)

  > Sub-period calibration with full multi-parameter dynamic schemes raises median NSE and LNSE to ~0.7–0.8 across 219 MOPEX catchments, versus ~0.4–0.5 for static parameterization. Dynamic catchment characteristics are extracted via a climate + land-surface index system, screened with MIC, reduced with PCA, and clustered into sub-periods (mean 4.2 per catchment) using Fuzzy C-Means. A smooth-transition strategy at sub-period boundaries (Experiment 7) recovers low-flow LNSE robustness relative to abrupt parameter switching, while FDC-based composite objectives paradoxically degraded performance by overfitting extreme quantiles. The framework is model-agnostic and applicable to lumped, semi-distributed, and distributed models, making it directly relevant to non-stationary catchments under vegetation change, land-use shifts, or climate variability.

- [ ] **[The rising threats of global wildland–human interface revealed by a scale-adaptive approach](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2604044123)** · Junnan Xia et al. · *PNAS* · [notes](../notes/W7157815305.md)

  > First global map to explicitly differentiate the Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) from the Wildland-Agriculture Interface (WAI) over 2000–2020, using a scale-adaptive HEALpix Quadtree algorithm. The global WHI covered 4.87% of terrestrial area in 2020, with WAI (4.68%) far exceeding WUI (0.19%). WUI expanded dramatically by 59.76% over two decades; WAI showed a net decrease of −3.26% but with high spatial turnover masking large compensating losses and gains. 5.03% of the world's key protected areas were encroached by WHI, and the type of interface (WUI vs. WAI) distinctly shaped both wildfire regimes and biodiversity gradients. Underscores the need for a global WHI monitoring network to mitigate rising threats to ecosystems and human security.

- [ ] **[Freezing-Enhanced Accumulation of ROS Generated by Fulvic Acid: Implications for As(III) Oxidation](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.est.5c18395)** · Wenxiu Qin et al. · *Environmental Science & Technology* · [notes](../notes/W7157629895.md)

  > Freezing concentrates reactive oxygen species (ROS) produced by fulvic acid, establishing freeze-enhanced ROS as the dominant oxidants of As(III) under cryogenic conditions. Redefines arsenic redox cycling in cold, humic-substance-rich environments by identifying freeze-thaw cycles as geochemically active windows for pollutant redox dynamics — not passive periods. Directly relevant to arsenic mobilization in seasonally frozen soils and cold-region groundwater systems.

- [ ] **[Earth Greening Directly Affects Regional Atmospheric Oxidation Chemistry Evidenced by Tibetan Plateau Aerosol Nitrate Isotopes](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.est.5c16954)** · P. Chen et al. · *Environmental Science & Technology* · [notes](../notes/W7158160980.md)

  > Aerosol nitrate isotope records from the Tibetan Plateau provide direct evidence that vegetation expansion (earth greening) modifies atmospheric oxidation chemistry in remote high-altitude regions with minimal anthropogenic interference. Identifies a vegetation–oxidant coupling as an underappreciated biogeochemical feedback with potential consequences for aerosol formation, nitrogen deposition, and climate feedbacks in high-altitude ecosystems undergoing rapid change.
