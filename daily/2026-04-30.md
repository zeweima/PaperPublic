- [ ] **[Catchment precipitation partitioning in the Budyko framework is controlled by root zone storage capacity](https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/ae612a)** · Ibrahim et al. · *Environmental Research Letters* · [notes](../notes/W7154954214.md)

  > Root zone storage capacity (S_r,max) is the dominant global control on Budyko ω-parameter deviations across >5,000 catchments (Spearman ρ = 0.68), exceeding all other tested controls and reaching ρ = 0.87 in cold and 0.83 in Mediterranean climates. The memory-method S_r,max links vegetation rooting depth adaptation directly to catchment-scale ET/runoff partitioning and supports its use as a physically grounded LSM parameterization target. Weaker relationships in arid climates suggest additional unresolved controls, and causality between vegetation adaptation and partitioning is not fully disentangled.

- [ ] **[Underestimated agricultural losses due to flooding](https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.aed2754)** · Zhang et al. · *Science Advances* · [notes](../notes/W7154479299.md)

  > A flood-stress algorithm coupling CaMa-Flood global floodplain hydrodynamics with GGCMI3 crop models corrects 21–29% yield biases under extreme wet conditions that standard crop model ensembles miss entirely. Under SSP585, projected annual US flood losses reach ~$287M (maize), $234M (soybean), and $212M (wheat), with flood damage surpassing drought in 27–55% of US states depending on crop. The framework is the first to show that flood mitigation is co-equal to drought adaptation for food security planning and is directly extensible to ISIMIP-type assessments.

- [ ] **[More eddying of subtropical western boundary currents boosts stratification and cools shelf seas](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41558-026-02599-9)** · Gunn & Beal · *Nature Climate Change* · [notes](../notes/W7154464084.md)

  > A 26-month mooring array at 34°S shows net eddy heat flux directed toward the Agulhas Current core, with ~40 submesoscale frontal instabilities driving baroclinic conversion of (4±2)×10⁻⁸ m²s⁻³ and cooling the adjacent shelf while surface waters warm at 3–4× mean-ocean rate. The result generalises: all subtropical western boundary currents are expected to drive increasing shelf-sea extremes as eddying intensifies, independent of whether volume transport holds steady. Increased upwelling may boost coastal productivity but also intensify ecological extremes.
