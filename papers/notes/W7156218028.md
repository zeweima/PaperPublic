# Methane leakage thresholds for net climate benefits of wastewater biogas recovery

**Authors:** Xiatong Li, Jun-Jie Zhu, Yuqing Yan et al.
**Venue:** Nature Sustainability · **Date:** 2026-04-27 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41893-026-01818-7
**Score:** 9/10 top pick
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

CH4 leakage rates of 0.4–65% across 50+ US wastewater facilities mean many already exceed net-zero thresholds; technology- and grid-specific safe limits are 2–10% of biogas produced.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Methane leakage during biogas digestion and handling at water resource recovery facilities (WRRFs) can negate climate benefits, but quantitative leakage thresholds have not been characterized at national scale.
- **Method:** US national-scale assessment compiling measured leakage data from 50+ facilities plus all available literature; regional- and technology-specific scenario and sensitivity analyses; evaluated heat recovery, grid emissions intensity, and biogas utilization pathways (combined heat and power vs. renewable natural gas).
- **Key result:** Observed leakage rates ranged 0.4–65% of biogas produced; net-zero emission thresholds span 2–10% depending on technology and grid mix. A large fraction of current systems already exceeds the net-zero threshold. Grid decarbonization makes renewable natural gas more viable than CHP in future low-carbon contexts.
- **Why it matters:** Provides a quantitative roadmap for leak detection and repair that can convert most facilities from climate liabilities to assets; makes the case for binding, performance-based methane monitoring and enforceable leakage thresholds in wastewater policy.
- **Caveats:** Reliant on literature-compiled leakage measurements that may have inconsistent measurement methods; scenarios depend on assumed grid decarbonization trajectories; US-centric — thresholds may shift in other regulatory or grid contexts.
