# The critical role of iron redox cycling in organic carbon dynamics of earthworm casts

**Authors:** Zheng Ni, Bin Jia, Xiangwei Wang et al.
**Venue:** Geoderma · **Date:** 2026-04-24 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.geoderma.2026.117827
**Score:** 9/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR
Fe(II)-driven hydroxyl radical production accounts for 26.9% of SOC mineralization in fresh earthworm casts, while Fe(II) oxidation to short-range ordered iron oxides shifts OM stabilization from adsorption to co-precipitation as casts age.

## Summary
- **Problem:** Earthworm casts are known SOC hotspots, but the abiotic role of reactive iron redox cycling — distinct from microbial activity — in controlling SOC mineralization and stabilization within casts was uncharacterized.
- **Method:** Fresh earthworm casts and adjacent bulk soil collected from a cropland Aridisol (Northwest China) were subjected to a 90-day laboratory incubation (n = 5 replicates, sampled at 9 time points). Reactive Fe(II), ROS (·OH, H₂O₂), CO₂ efflux, four-step sequential iron-OC extractions (WEOC, Fe_p-OC, Fe_o-OC, Fe_d-OC), FT-ICR-MS molecular characterization, and substrate-explicit thermodynamic modeling (ΔG_cox, λ) were combined. ·OH quenching (TBA) and Fenton addition experiments isolated the abiotic Fe-ROS contribution to mineralization.
- **Key result:** Reactive Fe(II) in fresh casts was 5.53× bulk soil (371.83 vs. ~67 mg kg⁻¹); ·OH was 3.34× bulk soil. TBA quenching reduced CO₂ emissions 86.2% in fresh (day 0) casts, confirming ·OH drove 26.9% of initial CO₂ flux. Over 90 days, Fe(II) oxidized to short-range ordered iron oxides (Fe_o), shifting OM stabilization from adsorption-dominated (~82% Fe_p-OC at day 0) to increasing co-precipitation (Fe_o-OC rose from 904.6 to 1295.3 mg kg⁻¹). FT-ICR-MS showed aged cast Fe_o-OC was enriched in lipid/protein-like and aminosugar compounds — bioavailable but mineral-shielded — while NOSC shifted from −1.245 to −0.648, indicating progressive oxidation.
- **Why it matters:** Establishes a previously overlooked abiotic–biotic coupling: Fe redox simultaneously accelerates short-term SOC loss (via ROS) and promotes long-term stabilization (via amorphous Fe-OC co-precipitation). In agroecosystems where earthworms consume 2–15 Mg ha⁻¹ yr⁻¹ of organic residues, this Fe-mediated mechanism represents a significant, cumulative influence on SOC stocks that models ignoring abiotic iron processes will underestimate.
- **Caveats:** Single site (Aridisol, semi-arid NW China) with one dominant earthworm species (*Metaphire guillelmi*); generalizability to other soil orders, wetter climates, and earthworm functional groups is untested. Incubation was conducted at 25°C under controlled moisture (80% WHC), which may not reflect field redox dynamics. Ecosystem-scale CO₂ flux attribution from ·OH remains model-dependent.
