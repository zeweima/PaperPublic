# Bidirectional feedbacks between Abies koreana dieback and soil properties slow soil carbon turnover in a subalpine forest

**Authors:** Hyunshin Koh, Yea-Na Han, Chanoh Park et al.
**Venue:** Biogeochemistry · **Date:** 2026-04-27 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10533-026-01332-0
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR
Korean fir dieback on nutrient-poor, sandy slopes slows soil organic matter decomposition, producing older SOC and elevated C:N ratios via suppressed rhizosphere priming.

## Summary
- **Problem:** Whether forest dieback responds to pre-existing soil conditions or actively alters soil biogeochemistry — and whether these relationships constitute a feedback loop — is poorly resolved for subalpine conifers.
- **Method:** Soil sampling at 0–5, 5–10, and 10–15 cm depths across 9 stands on Mt. Jiri, South Korea, stratified into high-mortality (HM) and low-mortality (LM) sites; analysis included mineralogical diversity, texture, nutrient content, volumetric soil water content, field capacity, soil C:N, δ¹⁵N, and SOC age (radiocarbon-inferred).
- **Key result:** HM sites occupy steeper southwestern slopes with lower mineralogical diversity and higher sand content. SOC at 0–5 and 5–10 cm is older, C:N is higher, and δ¹⁵N is more depleted relative to LM sites; volumetric water content and field capacity did not differ between groups.
- **Why it matters:** Dieback-induced suppression of rhizosphere exudation and litter-soil connectivity slows SOM decomposition, potentially stabilising carbon in the short term but signalling deteriorating nutrient cycling; this bidirectional feedback may amplify tree mortality under continued climate stress in montane ecosystems.
- **Caveats:** Causal direction is inferred from correlational sampling; temporal dynamics and microbial community data are absent from the abstract; results may be specific to the volcanic-origin soils of Mt. Jiri.
