# Bidirectional feedbacks between Abies koreana dieback and soil properties slow soil carbon turnover in a subalpine forest

**Authors:** Hyunshin Koh, Yea-Na Han, Chanoh Park et al.
**Venue:** Biogeochemistry · **Date:** 2026-04-27 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10533-026-01332-0
**Score:** 9/10 top pick
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR

Korean fir dieback on nutrient-poor, sandy slopes slows SOM decomposition via suppressed rhizosphere priming and reduced litter–soil connectivity.

## Summary

- **Problem:** Widespread subalpine conifer dieback is expanding globally, yet how tree mortality simultaneously responds to and alters soil biogeochemistry — and what this means for soil carbon fate — is poorly resolved.
- **Method:** Soil sampling at 0–5, 5–10, and 10–15 cm depths across nine stands (high-mortality HM vs. low-mortality LM) on Mt. Jiri, South Korea; measured mineralogy, texture, pH, volumetric water content, soil C:N, δ¹⁵N, and radiocarbon age of SOC.
- **Key result:** HM sites had steeper SW-facing slopes, lower mineralogical diversity, higher sand content, and nutrient-poor soils. Soil C:N was higher, δ¹⁵N more depleted across all depths, and SOC older at 0–5 and 5–10 cm in HM — consistent with slower SOM decomposition. Volumetric water content and field capacity did not differ, suggesting post-dieback organic matter accumulation compensated water retention.
- **Why it matters:** Bidirectional linkages between dieback and soil biogeochemistry mean that site-specific physicochemistry and rhizosphere suppression jointly constrain SOM turnover; models of soil carbon under increasing conifer dieback must incorporate both drivers.
- **Caveats:** Nine stands is a small sample; causal direction of some feedbacks (e.g., water retention enhancement) not experimentally confirmed; single mountain site limits generalizability.
