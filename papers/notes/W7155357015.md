# Sustainable global irrigation expansion could support only two-thirds of croplands under 1.5 °C and 3 °C warming

**Authors:** Liyin He, Lorenzo Rosa
**Venue:** Nature Food · **Date:** 2026-04-23 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s43016-026-01338-9
**Score:** 8/10<sup> top pick</sup>
**Source:** abstract

## TL;DR
Sustainable irrigation expansion can reach only ~two-thirds of global croplands under both 1.5 °C and 3 °C warming scenarios.

## Summary
- **Problem:** As climate warming intensifies drought stress on rainfed agriculture, irrigation expansion is a key adaptation option — but its sustainable upper bound (constrained by freshwater availability and ecosystem limits) relative to future cropland demand is unknown.
- **Method:** <unclear from abstract> — likely global hydrological / water-balance modeling combining crop water requirements under 1.5 °C and 3 °C warming with sustainable water supply limits across cropland extents worldwide.
- **Key result:** Sustainable irrigation expansion could support only ~two-thirds of croplands globally under both 1.5 °C and 3 °C warming, implying a substantial fraction of cropland (~one-third) cannot be viably irrigated even under optimal expansion scenarios.
- **Why it matters:** Sets a biophysical ceiling on irrigation-based climate adaptation, highlighting that a large share of global cropland will remain vulnerable to warming-driven water deficits regardless of infrastructure investment; other adaptation strategies (crop switching, demand reduction) are necessary for the unirrigable fraction.
- **Caveats:** Specific regional breakdowns, the definition of "sustainable" (e.g., groundwater depletion thresholds, environmental flow requirements), crop types considered, and socioeconomic feasibility constraints are unclear from the title alone; full quantitative details require access to the paper.
