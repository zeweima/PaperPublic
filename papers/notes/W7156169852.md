# A Geographically Weighted Gaussian Process Regression (GW-GPR) emulator of anthropogenic PM2.5 from the GEOS-Chem High Performance (GCHP) 13.0.0 global chemical transport model

**Authors:** Anthony Y. H. Wong, Sebastian D. Eastham, Erwan Monier et al.
**Venue:** Geoscientific Model Development · **Date:** 2026-04-27 · **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.5194/gmd-19-3335-2026
**Score:** 8/10 top pick
**Source:** full text

## TL;DR

GW-GPR emulates GCHP global PM2.5 at grid-cell scale with R²=0.94–0.99 across four policy scenarios in <10 s CPU time versus ~3000 CPU hours for GCHP.

## Summary

- **Problem:** GCHP requires ~3000 CPU hours per 1-year run, blocking use in integrated assessment; existing linearized source-receptor matrices cannot capture non-linear secondary aerosol chemistry.
- **Method:** 120 Latin Hypercube-sampled GCHP perturbation experiments; 7 emission scaling factors (NOₓ, SO₂, NH₃, NMVOC, BC, OC, CO) + surface CH₄ + atmospheric CO₂ as inputs; geographic Gaussian blurring to represent transport; GPR kernel = rational quadratic + Matérn 3/2 + white noise. Evaluated on four policy scenarios (2050 vs. 2014) and against the AerChemMIP ensemble.
- **Key result:** Cross-validation R²=0.97, MAE=0.356 µg m⁻³. On independent scenarios R²=0.94–0.99, MAE=0.20–0.42 µg m⁻³; 58.7–84.8% of grid cells agree within 1 emulator SD. Premature mortality impacts match GCHP within 1.2%. Runtime: ~10 CPU seconds per scenario. GPR outperforms multilinear regression by 0.05–0.10 µg m⁻³ MAE over US, Europe, South America.
- **Why it matters:** First global CTM emulator at grid-cell scale with explicit non-linearity and predictive uncertainty; enables air quality scenario screening in integrated assessment workflows without atmospheric science computing infrastructure. AerChemMIP validation confirms cross-model applicability.
- **Caveats:** Consistent high bias up to 2 µg m⁻³ over the Sahel and Bay of Bengal. Biogenic SOA excluded from anthropogenic PM2.5. Training uses globally uniform emission scaling, potentially underrepresenting extreme spatial heterogeneity.
